# Saudi PDPL — Personal Data Protection Law

## At a glance

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Instrument | Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) |
| Issuer | Kingdom of Saudi Arabia — Royal Decree M/19, 09/02/1443H (16 September 2021) |
| Material amendment | Royal Decree M/148, 05/09/1444H (27 March 2023) |
| Binding language | Arabic (official); English translation is reference only |
| Status | In force. Full enforcement (post grace period) from 14 September 2024 |
| Competent authority | Saudi Data and AI Authority (SDAIA) for private sector; NDMO for public-sector entities |
| Implementing Regulations | Issued by SDAIA, 7 September 2023 (Umm Al-Qura Gazette) |
| Personal Data Transfer Regulation | Issued by SDAIA, September 2024 |
| Relationship to other frameworks | PDPL is the primary personal-data law. NDMO Standards layer additional obligations for government entities (see NDMO-Standards.md). SAMA-supervised entities have SAMA-issued overlays but PDPL still applies to their personal-data processing. NCA ECC-2:2024 §2-7 is the cybersecurity-security control complement to PDPL's Art. 19. |
| Article 32 | Repealed by the 2023 amendment |

## Exact structure

| Level | Count |
|-------|-------|
| Articles | 43 (Article 32 repealed) |
| Chapters / groupings | No formal chapter headings in the text; structure follows: definitions (Art. 1), scope (Art. 2–3), rights (Art. 4–7), controller/processor obligations (Art. 8–31), specific processing types (Art. 23–29), supervision and enforcement (Art. 30, 33–40), confidentiality (Art. 41), implementing regs (Art. 42–43) |

> **Numbering note:** The PDPL uses article numbers only — there are no subdomain or sub-control codes. When citing in findings, use "PDPL Art. [N]" or "PDPL Art. [N]([paragraph])" for multi-paragraph articles. Where the Implementing Regulations add specificity, cite "PDPL IR Art. [N]" (verify article numbers against the Implementing Regulations document directly).

## Key definitions (PDPL Art. 1)

| Term | Definition (paraphrased from Art. 1) |
|------|---------------------------------------|
| Personal Data | Any data, regardless of source or form, that may lead to identifying a specific individual, or may directly or indirectly make identification possible. Includes name, national ID number, addresses, contact numbers, licence numbers, records, personal assets, bank and credit-card numbers, photos and videos, and any other data of a personal nature. |
| Sensitive Data | Personal data revealing racial or ethnic origin; religious, intellectual or political belief; security or criminal-conviction data; biometric data for identification purposes; genetic data; health data; data indicating one or both parents are unknown. (Art. 1(11)) |
| Genetic Data | Personal data related to hereditary or acquired characteristics that uniquely identify the physiological or health characteristics of a natural person, derived from biological sample analysis (e.g. DNA). (Art. 1(12)) |
| Health Data | Personal data related to an individual's physical, mental or psychological health condition, or health services received. (Art. 1(13)) |
| Credit Data | Personal data related to an individual's request for or obtaining of financing from a financing entity, their ability to obtain and repay debts, and credit history. (Art. 1(15)) |
| Processing | Any operation on personal data by any means (manual or automated): collecting, recording, saving, indexing, organising, formatting, storing, modifying, updating, consolidating, retrieving, using, disclosing, transmitting, publishing, sharing, linking, blocking, erasing or destroying. (Art. 1(5)) |
| Collection | Collection of personal data by the controller from the data subject directly, a representative, a legal guardian, or any other party — in accordance with the Law. (Art. 1(6)) |
| Disclosure | Enabling any person other than the controller or processor to access, collect or use personal data by any means and for any purpose. (Art. 1(8)) |
| Transfer | Transfer of personal data from one place to another for processing. (Art. 1(9)) |
| Destruction | Any action that renders personal data unreadable, irretrievable, and incapable of identifying the data subject. (Art. 1(7)) |
| Controller | Any public entity, natural person or private legal person that specifies the purpose and manner of processing personal data, whether processing is done by that controller or by a processor. (Art. 1(18)) |
| Processor | Any public entity, natural person or private legal person that processes personal data for the benefit and on behalf of the controller. (Art. 1(19)) |
| Data Subject | The individual to whom the personal data relates. (Art. 1(16)) |
| Public Entity | Any ministry, department, public institution or public authority, any independent public entity in the Kingdom, or any affiliated entity therewith. (Art. 1(17)) |

## Scope (PDPL Art. 2)

| Scope element | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Territorial | Applies to any processing of personal data of individuals in KSA, by any means (Art. 2(1)) |
| Extraterritorial | Applies to processing of personal data of KSA residents from outside the Kingdom (Art. 2(1)) |
| Deceased individuals | Included where data would identify the deceased or a family member (Art. 2(1)) |
| Excluded | Personal/family use processing where the data subject has not published or disclosed it to others (Art. 2(2)); the Regulations define personal and family use |

## Data subject rights (PDPL Art. 4)

| Right | Article | Key detail |
|-------|---------|------------|
| Right to be informed | Art. 4(1) | Legal basis and purpose of collection |
| Right of access | Art. 4(2) | Access to personal data held by the controller, subject to Art. 9 restrictions and Regulations |
| Right to data portability | Art. 4(3) | Obtain personal data in a readable and clear format (conditions in Regulations) |
| Right to correction / completion / update | Art. 4(4) | Request correction, completion, or updating of inaccurate data |
| Right to destruction | Art. 4(5) | Request destruction when data is no longer needed by the data subject — subject to Art. 18 retention obligations |
| Right to withdraw consent | Art. 5(2) | Data subject may withdraw consent at any time; controls set out in Regulations |

**Access restriction:** The controller may set timeframes and may limit access rights where: (a) necessary to protect the data subject or others from harm; or (b) the controller is a public entity and restriction is required for security, required by another law, or required for judicial purposes (Art. 9(1)). Access must be prevented altogether in the Art. 16 disclosure-prohibition situations (Art. 9(2)).

## Lawful bases for processing (PDPL Art. 5–6)

| Basis | Applies to | Article |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Consent of the data subject | All personal data | Art. 5(1) |
| Actual interest of data subject (where contact is impossible or difficult) | All personal data | Art. 6(1) |
| Legal obligation or prior agreement to which the data subject is a party | All personal data | Art. 6(2) |
| Public entity — security or judicial purposes | All personal data | Art. 6(3) |
| Legitimate interest of the controller — no prejudice to data-subject rights; no sensitive data | Non-sensitive personal data only | Art. 6(4) |

> **Key rule:** Consent cannot be made a condition of providing a service or benefit unless that service/benefit is directly related to the processing for which consent is sought (Art. 7).

> **Consent for sensitive data:** More restrictive. The Implementing Regulations specify when explicit consent is required and conditions for legal-guardian consent where the data subject lacks full legal capacity (Art. 5(1)).

## Collection principles (PDPL Art. 10–11)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Collect directly from the data subject by default | Art. 10 |
| Process only for the purpose for which data was collected | Art. 10 |
| Exceptions permitting collection from other sources or for secondary purposes | Art. 10(1)–(7): data subject consent; publicly available data; public entity security/judicial/public-interest; harm to data subject if complied with; public health/safety/life protection; anonymised data; legitimate interest of controller (no sensitive data) |
| Purpose must be directly related to the controller's activities and not contravene legal provisions | Art. 11(1) |
| Collection methods must not involve deception, misleading or extortion; must be direct, clear and secure | Art. 11(2) |
| Data minimisation — content must be appropriate and limited to the minimum necessary | Art. 11(3) |
| Cease collection and destroy data when it is no longer necessary for the collection purpose | Art. 11(4) |

## Controller obligations — full catalogue

### Privacy policy (Art. 12)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Implement a privacy policy | Art. 12 |
| Make the policy available to data subjects before collecting their personal data | Art. 12 |
| Policy must specify: purpose of collection; personal data to be collected; means of collection, processing, storage and destruction; data-subject rights and how to exercise them | Art. 12 |

### Notice at collection (Art. 13)

The controller must inform the data subject of all of the following at the time of direct collection:

| Item | Article |
|------|---------|
| Legal basis for collecting the personal data | Art. 13(1) |
| Purpose of collection; which data fields are mandatory vs optional; that data will not be processed inconsistently with the collection purpose except in Art. 10 situations | Art. 13(2) |
| Identity and address of the data collector (unless security purpose) | Art. 13(3) |
| Entities to which data will be disclosed; whether data will be transferred, disclosed or processed outside KSA | Art. 13(4) |
| Potential consequences of not providing the personal data | Art. 13(5) |
| Data-subject rights under Art. 4 | Art. 13(6) |
| Additional elements set by the Regulations based on the controller's activity | Art. 13(7) |

### Data quality (Art. 14)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Verify accuracy, completeness, timeliness and relevance to the collection purpose before processing | Art. 14 |

### Disclosure controls (Art. 15–16)

**Permitted disclosure bases (Art. 15):**

| Basis | Article |
|-------|---------|
| Data subject consent | Art. 15(1) |
| Publicly available source | Art. 15(2) |
| Public entity — public interest, security, other law, judicial requirement | Art. 15(3) |
| Protection of public health/safety or individual life/health | Art. 15(4) |
| Anonymised subsequent processing | Art. 15(5) |
| Legitimate interest of controller — no prejudice to data subject; no sensitive data | Art. 15(6) |

**Disclosure prohibitions (Art. 16) — even when Art. 15(1),(2),(5),(6) bases apply, disclosure is prohibited if it:**

| Prohibition | Article |
|------------|---------|
| Threatens security, harms or conflicts with interests of the Kingdom | Art. 16(1) |
| Affects the Kingdom's relations with any other state | Art. 16(2) |
| Prevents crime detection, affects fair trial rights, or affects integrity of criminal procedures | Art. 16(3) |
| Compromises the safety of an individual | Art. 16(4) |
| Violates the privacy of another individual (other than the data subject) | Art. 16(5) |
| Conflicts with interests of a person lacking legal capacity | Art. 16(6) |
| Violates legally established professional obligations | Art. 16(7) |
| Involves breach of an obligation, procedure, or judicial decision | Art. 16(8) |
| Exposes a confidential information source in a way detrimental to the public interest | Art. 16(9) |

### Accuracy and correction notification (Art. 17)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| When personal data is corrected, completed or updated, notify all entities to which data has been transferred | Art. 17(1) |
| Regulations set timeframes for correction, types of correction, and procedures to avoid consequences of processing incorrect data | Art. 17(2) |

### Retention and destruction (Art. 18)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Destroy personal data without undue delay when no longer necessary for the collection purpose | Art. 18(1) |
| Data may be retained after purpose ceases if it cannot identify the data subject (anonymised) — subject to Regulations | Art. 18(1) |
| **Mandatory retention exception (a):** Where a legal basis requires retention for a specific period → retain until that period lapses or the collection purpose is satisfied, whichever is longer; then destroy | Art. 18(2)(a) |
| **Mandatory retention exception (b):** Where data is closely related to a case before a judicial authority → retain until judicial procedures are concluded; then destroy | Art. 18(2)(b) |

### Security measures (Art. 19)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Implement all necessary organisational, administrative and technical measures to protect personal data | Art. 19 |
| Protective measures apply **including during the transfer of personal data** | Art. 19 |
| Controls and procedures set by the Regulations | Art. 19 |

### Breach notification (Art. 20)

| Obligation | Recipient | Article |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Notify the Competent Authority (SDAIA) upon becoming aware of any breach, damage or illegal access to personal data | SDAIA | Art. 20(1) |
| Notify the data subject if the breach, damage or illegal access would cause damage to their data or prejudice their rights and interests | Data subject | Art. 20(2) |
| Timelines and method set by the Implementing Regulations (72 hours to SDAIA where breach causes harm; without undue delay to data subject for high-risk breaches) | — | IR (verify against Regs) |

### Responding to data-subject requests (Art. 21)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Respond to data-subject rights requests within the period and in the method set out in the Regulations | Art. 21 |
| Regulations specify response timeframe (generally 30 days; extendable in defined circumstances — verify against Implementing Regulations) | IR |

### Data protection impact assessment (Art. 22)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Conduct impact assessment of personal data processing in relation to any product or service, based on the nature of the controller's activity | Art. 22 |
| Provisions and conditions set by the Regulations | Art. 22 |

### Processor governance (Art. 8)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Select only processors providing necessary guarantees to implement the PDPL and Regulations | Art. 8 |
| Monitor processor compliance with PDPL and Regulations | Art. 8 |
| Controller remains responsible towards the data subject and SDAIA regardless of processor delegation | Art. 8 |
| Regulations set out required provisions including sub-processor contract requirements | Art. 8 |

### Data protection officer (Art. 30(2))

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Appoint one or more personal data protection officers where required by the Regulations (thresholds: large-scale sensitive data processing; public-sector entities; regular and systematic monitoring) | Art. 30(2) |
| DPO responsibilities set in accordance with PDPL provisions | Art. 30(2) |

### Records of processing activities (Art. 31)

| Minimum record content | Article |
|------------------------|---------|
| Contact details of the controller | Art. 31(1) |
| Purpose of personal data processing | Art. 31(2) |
| Description of categories of data subjects | Art. 31(3) |

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|

## Specific processing types

### Health data (Art. 23)

Implementing Regulations add additional controls including:

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Restrict access to health data (incl. medical files) to the minimum number of employees/workers necessary to provide the required health services | Art. 23(1) |
| Restrict health data processing operations to the minimum extent necessary to provide health services or health insurance programmes | Art. 23(2) |

### Credit data (Art. 24)

Additional controls aligned with the Credit Information Law:

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Implement appropriate measures to verify explicit consent for collection, change of purpose, or disclosure/publication of credit data | Art. 24(1) |
| Notify the data subject when a disclosure request for their credit data is received | Art. 24(2) |

### Direct marketing (Art. 25–26)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Cannot use personal communication means (post, email) to send advertising/awareness materials without prior consent of the targeted recipient | Art. 25(1) |
| Must provide a clear mechanism for the recipient to opt out of further materials | Art. 25(2) |
| Exception: Public entities may send awareness materials without consent | Art. 25 preamble |
| Sensitive data must not be processed for marketing purposes | Art. 26 |
| Non-sensitive personal data may be used for marketing if collected directly from the data subject with consent | Art. 26 |

### Scientific, research and statistical purposes (Art. 27)

Processing without consent is permitted where:

| Condition | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Does not specifically identify the data subject | Art. 27(1) |
| Evidence of identity will be destroyed during processing before disclosure to any entity, and data is not sensitive | Art. 27(2) |
| Required by another law or in implementation of a prior agreement to which the data subject is a party | Art. 27(3) |

### Official documents (Art. 28)

| Obligation | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Prohibited to copy official documents where data subjects are identifiable, except where required by law or by a competent public authority pursuant to the Regulations | Art. 28 |

## Cross-border transfers (PDPL Art. 29 + Transfer Regulation)

### Permitted purposes (Art. 29(1))

Transfer or disclosure outside KSA is permissible for:

| Purpose | Article |
|---------|---------|
| Performance of an obligation under an agreement to which the Kingdom is a party | Art. 29(1)(A) |
| Serving the interests of the Kingdom | Art. 29(1)(B) |
| Performance of an obligation to which the data subject is a party | Art. 29(1)(C) |
| Other purposes set out in the Regulations | Art. 29(1)(D) |

### Mandatory conditions (Art. 29(2))

All Art. 29(1) transfers must also satisfy:

| Condition | Article |
|-----------|---------|
| Transfer must not prejudice national security or vital interests of the Kingdom | Art. 29(2)(A) |
| Adequate level of protection outside KSA — at least equivalent to PDPL/Regulations, assessed by the Competent Authority | Art. 29(2)(B) |
| Transfer limited to the minimum personal data needed | Art. 29(2)(C) |

### Life-or-health emergency exception (Art. 29(3))

The conditions in Art. 29(2) do not apply where the transfer is necessary to preserve life or vital interests of the data subject, or to prevent, examine, or treat disease.

### Transfer Regulation (September 2024)

The Personal Data Transfer Regulation operationalises Art. 29. Verify the following mechanisms against the published Regulation:

| Transfer mechanism | Notes |
|-------------------|-------|
| Adequacy decisions / approved jurisdictions list | SDAIA publishes and updates the list |
| Standard contractual clauses approved by SDAIA | Use SDAIA-approved form, not GDPR SCCs unless SDAIA has endorsed |
| Binding corporate rules / common binding rules | Subject to SDAIA review |
| Certification / codes of conduct | Recognised by SDAIA |
| Specific derogations (consent, contract performance, vital interests) | Art. 29(3) and Regulation |
| Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) | Expected for transfers to non-adequate jurisdictions; assesses laws and practices of the recipient country |

> **Verification note:** The Transfer Regulation was issued in September 2024. The specific list of adequate jurisdictions and approved SCC text must be retrieved from SDAIA's published instruments — do not assume GDPR adequacy lists map directly.

## Penalties (PDPL Art. 35–39)

| Offence | Penalty | Article |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Unlawful disclosure or publication of **sensitive data** with intention to harm the data subject or achieve personal benefit | Imprisonment up to 2 years **and/or** fine up to SAR 3,000,000 | Art. 35(1) |
| Recidivism for Art. 35 offence | Court may double the fine (not to exceed double the maximum) | Art. 35(4) |
| All other violations of PDPL or Regulations (persons with special natural or legal capacity) | Warning or fine up to SAR 5,000,000 | Art. 36(1) |
| Repeat violation of Art. 36(1) | Fine may be doubled (not to exceed double the maximum) | Art. 36(1) |
| Public-sector employees in violation | Disciplinary action per applicable disciplinary laws, without prejudice to Art. 35 / Art. 36 | Art. 39 |
| Publication of penalty decision | Competent court or committee may order publication of decision summary at violator's expense in local newspapers or other means | Art. 38(2) |
| Confiscation | Court may order confiscation of funds obtained from violations, without prejudice to bona fide third-party rights | Art. 38(1) |

**Enforcement process:** A committee of at least 3 members (including a technical specialist and a legal adviser) formed by SDAIA president's decision examines violations and issues warnings/fines. Decisions approved by SDAIA president or delegate. Decisions are appealable to the competent court (Art. 36(2)–(3)).

> **Note:** Verify the current enforcement-committee structure against latest SDAIA notices — enforcement practice has developed since the 2023 amendment.

## Audit hot spots — ranked by enforcement frequency

| Rank | Issue | Article / Ref | Why it gets cited |
|------|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | No privacy notice at collection point (web forms, mobile apps, physical forms) | Art. 12, 13 | Immediately visible to regulators and data subjects |
| 2 | Consent not granular — bundled T&C acceptance used as consent | Art. 5(1), IR | Implementing Regulations require specific conditions for valid consent |
| 3 | Records of processing activities (RoPA) absent or out of date | Art. 31 | First document SDAIA will request; frequently missing for acquired entities or legacy systems |
| 4 | No DPO where thresholds are met, or DPO without independence/proper remit | Art. 30(2) | SDAIA has prioritised DPO requirements in guidance |
| 5 | Vendors/processors without a written data-processing agreement | Art. 8 | Third-party processors endemic in cloud environments; often undocumented |
| 6 | No documented retention schedule — data kept indefinitely | Art. 11(4), 18 | Storage costs and residual risk, but also direct regulatory exposure |
| 7 | Cross-border transfers with no documented transfer mechanism or TIA | Art. 29, Transfer Reg | Particularly acute for multinationals using US/EU cloud infrastructure |
| 8 | Marketing databases processed without lawful basis review | Art. 25, 26 | Historical CRM data often lacks documented consent with timestamps |
| 9 | DPIA not conducted for high-risk processing (sensitive data, large-scale profiling, systematic monitoring) | Art. 22 | DPIA obligation frequently overlooked for new product launches |
| 10 | No documented breach response procedure or 72-hour notification path to SDAIA | Art. 20, IR | Incident response plans exist for cyber incidents but omit SDAIA notification workflow |
| 11 | Sensitive data processed for marketing purposes | Art. 26 | Health-adjacent commercial entities (insurance, wellness apps) routinely under-classify sensitive data |
| 12 | Data-subject rights requests unanswered or without documented SLA | Art. 21 | Response timelines set in Regulations; no tracking system means automatic non-compliance |
| 13 | Privacy notice does not disclose cross-border transfers or recipient entities | Art. 13(4) | Required disclosure frequently omitted from standard privacy policies |
| 14 | Official document copies taken without legal basis | Art. 28 | Common in onboarding workflows (ID copies) — basis must be documented |

## Common pitfalls

- **GDPR copy-paste:** Controllers with GDPR programmes often import GDPR artefacts directly. Specific divergences: PDPL has different lawful bases numbering, no legitimate interest for sensitive data, different transfer mechanics, SDAIA-specific registration and notification paths. GDPR artefacts are a starting point, not a finish line.
- **Consent as a catch-all:** Teams default to consent for all processing. Under PDPL, consent cannot be conditioned on receiving a service (Art. 7). This invalidates large volumes of legacy consent collected as a click-through in T&Cs.
- **Sensitive data under-classification:** Credit data, location data, and health-adjacent data (wellness, fitness) are frequently not classified as sensitive. The Art. 1(11) definition is broader than GDPR's Art. 9 categories.
- **Processor ≠ sub-processor management:** Art. 8 requires monitoring of processor compliance and covers sub-processor contracting. Many DPAs address primary processors only.
- **RoPA scope:** Art. 31 RoPA is often built only for the main product/service. Legacy systems, HR data, customer service platforms, and analytics tools are routinely omitted.
- **72-hour clock:** PDPL breach notification clock starts when the controller **becomes aware** — not when the incident is investigated or confirmed. Teams that route all incidents through a lengthy triage before notification breach the timeline.
- **Destruction vs anonymisation:** Art. 18(1) allows retention after purpose ceases only if anonymised such that the data subject cannot be identified. Pseudonymisation alone is insufficient under the PDPL definition.
- **Public-entity confusion:** NDMO, not SDAIA, is the primary supervisor for government entities' data processing. Public entities still implement PDPL obligations but report to NDMO. Failure to distinguish the right regulator leads to notifications going to the wrong body.
