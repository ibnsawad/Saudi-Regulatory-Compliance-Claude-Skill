# NCA CSCC-1:2019 — Critical Systems Cybersecurity Controls

## At a glance

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Issuer | National Cybersecurity Authority (NCA), KSA |
| Document | Critical Systems Cybersecurity Controls (CSCC-1:2019) |
| Status | Mandatory for systems formally designated as critical |
| Relationship to ECC | CSCC is an overlay; ECC remains baseline |

## Critical-system designation

CSCC applies to **systems**, not to whole entities. A system enters CSCC scope when it is designated critical because its compromise, disruption, or loss of integrity would have a major impact on:

- National security
- Public safety
- The national economy
- Essential government or public services
- Critical national infrastructure

Designation is typically made by the entity in coordination with NCA / sectoral regulator. Common candidates: SCADA in energy and water utilities, core banking and payment switches (where outside SAMA scope), national identity and population systems, healthcare core systems, telecom signalling networks, aviation / port operations.

If a user has not formally designated their critical systems, the first action is to run a designation exercise — many CSCC findings stem from absent designation.

## Domain structure

CSCC-1:2019 mirrors ECC's main-domain structure with stricter controls applied only to the in-scope critical systems:

1. **Cybersecurity Governance** — dedicated critical-system risk management, segregated governance, board oversight.
2. **Cybersecurity Defence** — strict IAM and PAM; comprehensive logging and 24/7 SOC monitoring; advanced threat detection (EDR/XDR, NDR); strong network segmentation and isolation; zero-trust principles; secure configuration baselines; vulnerability and patch management with stricter SLAs; cryptography with strong algorithms and key custody; physical security; secure software lifecycle for in-house and procured software; penetration testing on a defined cycle; advanced email and web protection.
3. **Cybersecurity Resilience** — tested DR and BC for critical systems; recovery-time and recovery-point objectives appropriate to criticality; resilient architecture (no single points of failure); continuity of monitoring during incidents.
4. **Third-Party Cybersecurity** — strict vendor security; supply-chain assurance; restrictions on remote vendor access.

## Stricter than ECC — what to check

For each in-scope critical system:

- **PAM**: every privileged session brokered, recorded, and reviewed. No standing privilege.
- **MFA**: phishing-resistant MFA for all administrative and remote access; no SMS / voice OTP for criticals.
- **Logging**: comprehensive coverage with extended retention; tamper-evident log storage.
- **Monitoring**: continuous, 24/7, with documented SOC use cases for critical-system threats and tested response runbooks.
- **Segmentation**: critical systems isolated from corporate networks; jump hosts; controlled break-out paths.
- **Vulnerability management**: strict SLAs (often days, not weeks) for critical-severity vulnerabilities.
- **Patch management**: change-controlled but not slow; emergency patching paths defined.
- **Pen testing**: at least annually for the critical system; before major changes.
- **DR**: regularly tested; documented evidence; alternative site or cloud DR with sovereignty constraints.
- **Vendors**: strictly governed remote access; jump hosts; recorded sessions; minimal standing access.

## Audit hot spots

- "We have a SOC" but no critical-system-specific use cases
- PAM tool deployed but bypass paths exist (local accounts, emergency accounts not vaulted)
- Network segmentation drawn on diagrams but not enforced (flat L2 in practice)
- DR plan exists but has not been tested in 12+ months
- Vendor remote access via VPN with shared credentials
- No threat intel feed tuned to the entity's critical systems

## Required artefacts

- Designation register: critical systems list, designation rationale, sign-off
- System-specific risk assessments and treatment plans
- PAM evidence, including session recordings and reviews
- SOC use-case catalogue and alert tuning evidence
- Pen-test reports and remediation evidence
- DR test reports and recovery objective evidence
- Vendor inventory with risk ratings, contracts, and access logs

## Mapping

- ECC-2:2024 — used as baseline; CSCC adds depth
- OTCC-1:2022 — for OT components within critical systems
- ISO/IEC 27001:2022 + Annex A
- NIST SP 800-82 (OT) and NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 high baseline
- IEC 62443 series for industrial automation

## Verification note

CSCC predates ECC-2:2024 and CCC-2:2024. Its language references ECC-1 in places, and NCA has indicated continued mapping work. When citing, use "CSCC-1:2019" and align findings to current ECC-2:2024 references where possible. If the user mentions a newer CSCC version, ask them to share the document — only act on it once the version is confirmed.
