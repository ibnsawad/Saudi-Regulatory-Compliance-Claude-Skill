## Purpose

CGEC-1:2019 was developed by NCA after reviewing multiple national and international cybersecurity e-commerce guidelines, studying related national initiatives, statistics, and regulatory requirements, and analysing previous cybersecurity incidents and attacks on consumers. It supports the national transformation program's goals aligned with Vision 2030, where e-commerce was identified as a key economic pillar.

The document assists consumers in the Kingdom around best practices to secure their devices, systems, online accounts, data, and payments when using online e-commerce channels.

---

## Context: KSA e-commerce landscape (as stated in CGEC)

- Saudi Arabia is one of the largest e-commerce markets in the MENA region.
- Approximately 58% of the population had shopped online at least once every three months (CITC study, 2017), spending an average of SAR 4,000 on online shopping annually.
- A significant portion of the Saudi population purchased from GCC and international e-commerce providers; only approximately 7% purchased exclusively from Saudi-based providers.
- The E-commerce Law was approved by the Council of Ministers to enhance reliability, increase economic contribution, and motivate e-commerce activities in the Kingdom.

## Scope of applicability

These guidelines target **all consumers in Saudi Arabia** conducting e-commerce through any channel (e.g., social media, websites, apps) using any computing device (e.g., personal computers, tablets, smartphones, and TVs). They are for **awareness purposes only** and are intended to help consumers navigate a secure e-commerce experience. They do not impose compliance obligations on organisations.

> **Note for compliance advisors:** CGEC is consumer guidance, not an organisational controls framework. E-commerce platform operators and merchants in KSA are subject to the E-commerce Law, PDPL (if processing personal data), and ECC-2:2024 (if NCA-scoped). CGEC may be referenced to demonstrate consumer-protection awareness when regulators review operator practices, but it is not itself an organisational compliance standard.

## Key guidelines per category — obligations table

### Category 1 — Protect Your E-commerce Accounts and Devices

| Ref | Guideline | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| 1-1 | Use Anti-Virus Software | Install anti-virus on all devices used for e-commerce before conducting any transaction; use only trusted, official anti-virus products |
| 1-2 | Use Different Passwords | Use a unique password per app, website, device, and social media account; change passwords periodically; never share passwords |
| 1-3 | Create Strong Passwords | Avoid common words, personal information, and sequential numbers; do not write passwords down; use long, complex combinations of special characters (@#$%^&), upper/lower case, and random numbers |
| 1-4 | Consider Using Additional Verification Options | Enable additional verification mechanisms (e.g., email or SMS confirmation) where offered by e-commerce platforms; prefer platforms that offer such extra verification protection |
| 1-5 | Update Applications Regularly | Keep all applications — including the operating system and antivirus — up to date; enable automatic updates or schedule manual updates at a fixed weekly/monthly interval |
| 1-6 | Back Up Your Data | Copy data to a computer or external hard drive to prevent loss from malware infection; enable automatic backup if available |
| 1-7 | Change Default Settings | Review and change default security and privacy settings on accounts and browsers; avoid saving social media login credentials, payment data, or bank account data in browsers |
| 1-8 | Pay Attention to Security Warnings | Do not ignore browser or system security warnings (e.g., alerts about unverified service providers or non-secure websites); read and understand them before proceeding |

### Category 2 — Secure Your E-commerce Transactions

| Ref | Guideline | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| 2-1 | Review Your Transactions Periodically | Periodically review credit card statements and eWallet transaction histories; immediately call the bank upon detecting suspicious or fraudulent purchases; stop compromised cards promptly via bank app or website; keep bank contact information current; enable SMS notifications for all account transactions |
| 2-2 | Use a Dedicated Credit Card | Use a single dedicated credit card exclusively for e-commerce purchases if multiple cards are held; request a low-limit or pre-paid card from the bank if available |
| 2-3 | Secure Your Electronic Wallet | Protect eWallet access with strong authentication (e.g., strong password); regularly check eWallet account for suspicious activities such as failed login attempts |

### Category 3 — Exercise Caution When Communicating Online for E-commerce

| Ref | Guideline | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| 3-1 | Protect Against Spam | Activate email spam filters; report spam messages (email or social media) immediately to the relevant platform; dedicate a separate email account for e-commerce use |
| 3-2 | Be Aware of Fraudulent Calls | Exercise caution with calls from unknown parties claiming to be e-commerce service providers; verify seller authenticity and reputation using verified platforms such as Ma'aroof (maroof.sa); prefer verified service providers |
| 3-3 | Use Secure Websites | Before entering personal or financial details, verify the URL starts with https:// (not http://); avoid platforms that do not use HTTPS, as it indicates absence of trusted encryption |
| 3-4 | Be Aware of Suspicious URLs | Avoid clicking links received via email, SMS, or social media (especially in advertisements) that redirect to unknown or fake websites; be alert to fake websites offering unrealistically low prices |
| 3-5 | Shop Using Secure Apps, Devices and Networks | Download apps only from official app stores to avoid fakes; avoid conducting e-commerce transactions on public devices (e.g., hotel business-centre computers) or over public Wi-Fi (e.g., airport networks) |
| 3-6 | Report Incidents Quickly | If an e-commerce account is hacked, notify the merchant immediately; if payment card data is stolen, contact the bank immediately; if a device is compromised, contact local authorities; early reporting limits damages |

### Category 4 — Limit Sharing Your Personal Information

| Ref | Guideline | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| 4-1 | Read the Privacy Policy | Shop only with reputable providers who publish a privacy policy; verify the policy clearly explains how data will be used and stored; avoid providers whose policies permit sharing personal data with third parties (e.g., advertising agencies) without consumer consent |
| 4-2 | Protect Your Personal Information | Provide only the minimum personal information necessary for a transaction; do not save or store personal or financial information on apps or websites; avoid sharing credit card details via non-secure channels such as email or social media |
| 4-3 | Ignore Phishing Messages | Verify the sender's identity before opening messages; be alert to phishing — messages disguised as legitimate entities seeking usernames, passwords, or payment details |
| 4-4 | Limit Permissions Granted to Apps | Carefully consider whether mobile apps or websites require permissions they request (location, camera, address book, microphone); limit app permissions to what is functionally necessary |

## Relevance for e-commerce platform operators and merchants

Although CGEC is consumer-facing, compliance teams advising KSA e-commerce operators should be aware of implied platform obligations:

| Consumer Guideline | Implied Platform Obligation | Relevant Regulatory Hook |
|--------------------|-----------------------------|--------------------------|
| 1-4 (Additional Verification) | Platforms should offer MFA / OTP options | ECC-2:2024 §2-2 (Identity and Access Management) |
| 1-8 (Security Warnings) | Platforms must implement HTTPS and valid TLS certificates; browser warnings indicate non-compliance | ECC-2:2024 §2-6 (Network Security) |
| 2-1 (Transaction Review) | Platforms must provide adequate transaction history and SMS alert features | E-commerce Law; SAMA rules for payment platforms |
| 3-3 / 3-4 (HTTPS / Suspicious URLs) | Platforms must use HTTPS; domain spoofing defences (DMARC, DKIM, SPF) reduce fake-URL incidents | ECC-2:2024 §2-4-3-5 (email authentication); E-commerce Law |
| 4-1 (Privacy Policy) | All e-commerce platforms processing personal data must publish a PDPL-compliant privacy notice | PDPL Art. 11 (Notice obligations); PDPL Implementing Regulations |
| 4-2 (Minimum Data) | Platforms must apply data minimisation | PDPL Art. 6 (Lawful basis); PDPL Implementing Regulations §4 |
| 3-6 (Report Incidents) | Platforms must have incident response channels for consumers and must internally report to NCA / SDAIA | ECC-2:2024 §2-13; PDPL Art. 20 (breach notification) |

## Evidence expectations (for operators benchmarking against CGEC)

| Guideline Area | What an Auditor or Regulator May Request |
|----------------|------------------------------------------|
| HTTPS / TLS | SSL/TLS certificate scan results; web-application security test output; no http:// redirect found on checkout pages |

| Privacy policy | Published privacy notice URL; PDPL review sign-off; date of last update; process for obtaining consumer consent |
| Transaction alerts | Screenshot / demo of SMS and email notification system; consumer notification policy |
| Consumer incident channel | Support portal or hotline details; incident response procedure showing consumer-facing escalation path |
| Data minimisation | Data flow map showing which fields are collected and justification for each; sign-up form screenshot |
| Email authentication | DMARC, DKIM, SPF DNS record verification output (e.g., MXToolbox results) |
| App permissions | Mobile app permission manifest audit; privacy impact assessment for each permission |
| Seller verification | Ma'aroof registration certificate or equivalent government-recognised seller verification record |

## Common pitfalls

1. **Conflating CGEC with a mandatory controls framework.** CGEC is consumer awareness guidance. Organisations cannot be fined for CGEC non-compliance directly — enforcement comes from PDPL, ECC, and the E-commerce Law. CGEC's value for operators is as a consumer-expectation benchmark and as a gap-analysis input for the broader regulatory stack.

2. **Ignoring PDPL obligations implied by CGEC.** Every guideline in Category 4 (Limit Sharing Your Personal Information) has a direct PDPL counterpart. Operators who design platforms at odds with Category 4 are likely to be non-compliant with PDPL data minimisation and notice obligations.

3. **Treating HTTPS as sufficient.** CGEC §3-3 recommends HTTPS; but TLS configuration quality matters. A valid certificate with deprecated cipher suites (TLS 1.0/1.1) or expired certificates provides cosmetic HTTPS without actual security.

4. **Missing the Ma'aroof reference.** CGEC §3-2 explicitly references Ma'aroof (maroof.sa) as the recommended platform for verifying seller authenticity. E-commerce operators operating without a Ma'aroof listing — or with an unverified one — face both consumer-trust and potential regulatory scrutiny under the E-commerce Law.

5. **Assuming mobile app stores guarantee security.** CGEC §3-5 recommends official app stores; however, operators still bear responsibility for ensuring their official app is discoverable and that counterfeit apps are actively removed through takedown processes.

6. **No eWallet security documentation.** Operators who provide eWallet functionality are expected (per §2-3) to offer strong authentication. Absence of password/biometric controls on eWallets is a high-impact gap given growing eWallet usage in KSA.

7. **Incident reporting confusion.** CGEC §3-6 tells consumers to report to the merchant, the bank, and local authorities. Operators must have clearly documented consumer-facing incident channels AND internal channels to NCA (ECC §2-13) and SDAIA (PDPL Art. 20). Both chains must exist and be tested.

## Key definitions (from CGEC Appendix A)

| Term | CGEC Definition |
|------|----------------|
| Attack | Any kind of malicious activity attempting to achieve unauthorized access, collection, disabling, prevention, destruction, or sabotage of information system resources or the information itself |
| Backup | Files, devices, data, and procedures available for use in case of failure, loss, deletion, or suspension of their original copies |
| Cybersecurity | Per Royal Decree 6801 (11/2/1439H): protection of networks, IT systems, operational technology systems and their hardware, software, services, and data from any penetration, disruption, modification, access, use, or unauthorized exploitation; includes information security and digital security |
| E-commerce Service Provider | Merchant (bound by commercial registration and using e-commerce) or practitioner (not bound by commercial registration but using e-commerce) |
| HTTPS | A protocol using encryption to secure web pages and data during network transmission; the secure version of HTTP |
| Incident | A compromise through violation of cybersecurity policies, acceptable use policies, practices, or cybersecurity controls or requirements |
| Phishing | The attempt to obtain sensitive information (usernames, passwords, credit card details) for malicious reasons by disguising as a trustworthy entity in email, text, or social media messages |
| Privacy | Freedom from unauthorized interference or disclosure of personal information about an individual |
| Threat | Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organisational operations, assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification, or denial of service; also the potential for a threat source to successfully exploit a particular vulnerability |

## Related frameworks

| Framework | Relationship to CGEC |
|-----------|----------------------|
| ECC-2:2024 | Organisational cybersecurity baseline that e-commerce operators must comply with; many CGEC consumer recommendations map to operator-side ECC controls (IAM, network security, incident management) |
| PDPL (Royal Decree M/19, 2021) + Implementing Regulations | Legal foundation for consumer data-protection rights implied by CGEC Category 4; operators must comply regardless of CGEC |
| E-commerce Law (KSA) | Primary regulatory instrument for e-commerce operators; CGEC was developed as a companion consumer-awareness document |
| NDMO Data Management Standards | Government-entity data handling standards; relevant for government-operated e-commerce portals |
| CST Cloud Computing Regulatory Framework (CCRF) | Applies to cloud-based e-commerce platforms and their hosting providers |
| OSMACC-1:2021 | NCA social-media account security controls; relevant to the social-media commerce channel referenced in CGEC §3-1 and §3-2 |
| Ma'aroof Platform (maroof.sa) | Government seller-verification registry explicitly referenced in CGEC §3-2; operated by the Ministry of Commerce |
