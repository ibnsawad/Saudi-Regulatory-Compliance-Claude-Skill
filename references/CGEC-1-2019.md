## Purpose and Rationale

CGEC-1:2019 was developed by NCA after reviewing multiple national and international cybersecurity
e-commerce guidelines, related national initiatives, statistics, and regulatory requirements, and
analysing previous cybersecurity incidents and attacks on consumers. It supports Vision 2030, where
e-commerce was identified as a key economic pillar, and the E-commerce Law approved by the Council
of Ministers to enhance reliability and increase e-commerce's contribution to the national economy.

The document assists consumers in the Kingdom around best practices to **secure their devices, systems,
online accounts, data, and payments** during online e-commerce activities.

---

## KSA E-commerce Context (as stated in CGEC source)

- Saudi Arabia is one of the largest e-commerce markets in the MENA region.
- Approximately 58% of the KSA population had shopped online at least once every three months
  (CITC study, 2017), spending an average of SAR 4,000 on online shopping annually.
- A significant portion purchased from GCC and international providers; only ~7% purchased
  exclusively from Saudi-based e-commerce providers.
- Growth drivers: convenience of home delivery, time savings, attractive internet offers, wide
  product range — but these also created new threat types that CGEC addresses.

---

## Scope of Applicability

These guidelines target **all consumers in Saudi Arabia** conducting e-commerce through any channel
(e.g., social media, websites, apps) using any computing device (e.g., personal computers, tablets,
smartphones, and TVs). They are **for awareness purposes** and are intended to help consumers navigate
a secure e-commerce experience.

> **Compliance advisor note:** CGEC is consumer guidance, not an organisational controls framework.
> It does not impose compliance obligations on individuals. E-commerce platform operators and merchants
> in KSA are subject to the **E-commerce Law**, **PDPL** (if processing personal data), and
> **ECC-2:2024** (if NCA-scoped). CGEC may be referenced to demonstrate consumer-protection awareness
> when regulators review operator practices — particularly obligations to display privacy policies,
> offer secure payment channels, and provide verified contact details — but CGEC is not itself an
> auditable organisational standard. The companion document **CGESP-1:2019** addresses obligations
> and guidance directed at e-commerce service providers.

## Category 1 — Protect Your E-commerce Accounts and Devices

---

### 1-1 Use Anti-Virus Software

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Use anti-virus software on all your devices, especially the ones you often use for e-commerce.
  If your device does not have a pre-installed antivirus software, it is recommended that you
  install one before conducting any e-commerce transaction.
- Make sure you use trusted and official versions of anti-virus software products.

**Operator implication:** E-commerce platforms must not deliver content that interferes with or
disables consumer anti-virus tools. Malvertising served through a platform constitutes a cybersecurity
failure under ECC-2:2024 and the E-commerce Law.

---

### 1-2 Use Different Passwords

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- If you are shopping on multiple apps or websites, use a different password for each. Similarly,
  use different passwords for your devices and social media accounts.
- Also, to protect your digital identity, make sure to change your passwords periodically and
  never share your passwords with others.

**Operator implication:** Platforms must not store or transmit passwords in plaintext. Password
policies for consumer accounts must enforce complexity and prohibit reuse — an obligation that maps
to ECC-2:2024 §2-2 and PDPL data security requirements.

---

### 1-3 Create Strong Passwords

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- When creating a password for your e-commerce account, follow these best practices:
  - Avoid using common everyday words (e.g., "password", "qwerty"), personal information
    (e.g., date of birth, graduation year) or sequential numbers (e.g., "123456").
  - Do not write it down.
  - Make it as long and complicated as possible, using combination of special characters
    (such as @#$%^&), upper case and lower-case letters and random numbers.

**Operator implication:** E-commerce platforms must enforce minimum password complexity at account
registration. Allowing weak passwords when stronger passwords are technically feasible contradicts
this guidance and exposes the operator to reputational and regulatory risk under the E-commerce Law.

---

### 1-4 Consider Using Additional Verification Options

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- If available, consider using additional verification mechanisms (such as email messages) offered
  by e-commerce apps or websites (including social media accounts).
- Choose to shop online with e-commerce service providers who provide that extra verification
  protection option.

**Operator implication:** Operators are strongly encouraged to offer multi-factor authentication (MFA)
or step-up verification for high-value or unusual transactions. Absence of any additional verification
option is a consumer-protection gap under the E-commerce Law and may be raised by regulators.
For operators also subject to SAMA, MFA is mandatory per the SAMA Cybersecurity Framework.

---

### 1-5 Update Applications Regularly

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Keep all your applications (including operating system and antivirus software) on your devices
  up to date. This can be done either automatically (by enabling that feature on your device) or
  manually (by dedicating a fixed time of the week/month to run the update).

**Operator implication:** E-commerce operators must keep their own platform software, APIs, and
third-party components patched and updated. Unpatched platform vulnerabilities that result in
consumer data exposure trigger PDPL breach notification obligations and ECC-2:2024 vulnerability
management requirements.

---

### 1-6 Back Up Your Data

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Back up your data by, for example, copying it to your computer or to an external hard drive to
  avoid losing your data in case of your device (which you use for e-commerce) got infected with
  viruses.
- Enable the automatic back up feature, if available on your device.

**Operator implication:** Platform operators must maintain their own backup and recovery capabilities
per ECC-2:2024 §2-10 (Backup and Recovery Management). Consumer data lost due to operator failure
to maintain adequate backups constitutes a data integrity breach under PDPL.

---

### 1-7 Change Default Settings on Your Devices and Accounts

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Review and change the default security and privacy settings on your accounts and web browser
  to, for example, avoid saving social media login credentials, payment or bank account data.

**Operator implication:** Platforms must not default account settings to insecure or over-permissive
states. Privacy-by-default is a PDPL principle (PDPL Art. 5 — processing must be limited to
what is necessary). Pre-checked consent boxes, default opt-in to data sharing, and auto-save of
payment credentials without explicit consent are non-compliant practices under PDPL.

---

### 1-8 Pay Attention to Security Warnings

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Do not ignore security warnings (e.g., alerts of unverified e-commerce service providers or
  non-secure websites) that may come up on your screen.
- Make sure that you read and understand them before taking any further actions.

**Operator implication:** Platforms must not suppress, override, or circumvent browser security
warnings. Serving content over HTTP (not HTTPS) such that browsers display security warnings is
a failure under ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System Protection) and the E-commerce Law's
requirement for secure transaction channels.

## Category 2 — Secure Your E-commerce Transactions

---

### 2-1 Review Your Transactions Periodically

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Review your bank (specifically your credit card) statements and electronic wallets (eWallets)
  transactions periodically. Call your bank right away if you see any suspicious fraudulent
  purchases. Stop your credit card immediately, through, for example, your bank's app or website,
  when your card is stolen. The sooner an online fraud is detected, the faster other consumers
  can also be alerted by the bank.
- Make sure your contact information with your bank is up to date.
- Enable SMS notifications on your credit card/eWallet to stay informed about any transactions
  on your account.

**Operator implication:** Platforms must provide consumers with clear, timely, accessible transaction
histories. Suppressing or delaying transaction notifications removes the consumer's ability to
detect fraud — an obligation under the E-commerce Law. SAMA-licensed payment entities must provide
real-time or near-real-time transaction alerts per SAMA regulatory requirements.

---

### 2-2 Use a Dedicated Credit Card

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- If you own multiple credit cards, try to use a dedicated one for e-commerce purchases.
- If offered by your bank, use a low limit card or a pre-paid one.

**Operator implication for banks/fintechs:** SAMA-licensed entities offering virtual or prepaid
cards for e-commerce are supporting this guidance. Failure to offer such products while competitors
do may be noted in supervisory reviews under SAMA's consumer-protection mandate.

---

### 2-3 Secure Your Electronic Wallet

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Make sure to protect access to your eWallet with a strong authentication mechanism (e.g.,
  password). eWallets are becoming more popular and widely used by consumers, therefore attacks
  related to these payment methods are rising.
- Frequently check your eWallet account for suspicious activities (e.g., failed login attempts).

**Operator implication:** eWallet operators must enforce strong authentication (SAMA CSF requirement),
provide visibility into failed login attempts, and notify consumers of suspicious activity. Under PDPL,
account compromise events that expose personal or financial data trigger breach notification
obligations.

## Category 3 — Exercise Caution When Communicating Online for E-commerce

---

### 3-1 Protect Your Account Against Unwanted Messages (Spam)

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Activate the spam filter on your email. Many email services (especially web email) have a
  filter included.
- Report spam messages (email/social media) immediately to your email provider/social media
  platform.
- Dedicate an email account to be used for e-commerce.

**Operator implication:** E-commerce operators must not send unsolicited commercial communications
without consent — a direct obligation under the E-commerce Law and PDPL (marketing requires a
lawful basis including, in most cases, prior consent). Operators must provide a functional
unsubscribe mechanism.

---

### 3-2 Be Aware of Fraudulent Calls

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Be aware of fraudulent calls. A common scam is fraudulent calls from fake e-commerce service
  providers who normally have fake websites/social media profiles.
- Make it a strong preference to shop and interact only with verified e-commerce service providers.
  Online review platforms (such as Ma'aroof — https://maroof.sa) are a good way to check an
  e-commerce seller's authenticity and reputation.

**Operator implication:** Legitimate e-commerce operators must maintain their registration and
verification on the Ma'aroof platform (operated by the Ministry of Commerce) as required by the
E-commerce Law. Absence from Ma'aroof or a poor Ma'aroof rating is a regulatory and consumer-trust
risk. Operators must also have documented anti-fraud and anti-impersonation procedures.

---

### 3-3 Use Secure Websites

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Before you enter your personal or financial details, make sure that the website's URL starts
  with (https://) instead of (http://).
- Avoid e-commerce service providers who do not offer this enhanced security, as this security
  feature means the website is using trusted encryption protocols to protect your personal and
  financial information.

**Operator implication:** HTTPS with a valid TLS certificate is a baseline technical requirement
for any e-commerce platform. Operating an e-commerce platform over HTTP, or using expired/self-signed
certificates, is non-compliant with ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System Protection), the NCS-1:2020
(MODERATE TLS requirements), and the E-commerce Law's secure-transaction obligation. Auditors treat
any HTTP-only payment page as a critical finding.

---

### 3-4 Be Aware of Suspicious URLs

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Avoid clicking on links you see on messages (email, SMS, or social media) or web (usually in
  advertisements), as these may redirect you to fake websites.
- Be aware of fake websites which, for example, offer unrealistic prices for products.

**Operator implication:** Operators must protect their brand from domain squatting and typosquatting
(OSMACC-1:2021 impersonation controls apply if the operator is government or CNI-adjacent). Platform
operators must not engage in misleading advertising practices that violate the E-commerce Law. Brand
monitoring (as required by OSMACC §2-6-1-3 for government entities) is best practice for all
significant e-commerce operators.

---

### 3-5 Shop Using Secure Apps, Devices and Networks

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Be aware of fake apps and websites. Those can be avoided by, for example, downloading apps
  from the official app stores.
- It is highly recommended to avoid conducting e-commerce transactions or entering your social
  media credentials on public devices (e.g., business center computers at hotels) or connecting
  to public Wi-Fi networks (e.g., airport Wi-Fi).

**Operator implication:** Operators must publish their apps exclusively through official app stores
(Apple App Store, Google Play Store) and must actively report counterfeit/fake apps to the platform
providers. Failure to take down fraudulent apps that impersonate the operator's brand is a regulatory
and consumer-protection risk. Mobile app security must satisfy ECC-2:2024 §2-3 and §2-4.

---

### 3-6 Report Incidents Quickly

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- If your e-commerce account has been hacked, act fast and inform the merchant.
- If your payment card information has been stolen, contact your bank immediately.
- If your device has been compromised, contact local authorities. The earlier you report any
  incident, the more you can limit damages.

**Operator implication:** Operators must provide a clearly visible, functional channel for consumers
to report account compromise and suspected fraud — an E-commerce Law requirement. Platforms must
have a documented incident response procedure. Under PDPL Art. 20, personal-data breaches that may
cause harm require notification to SDAIA without delay (Implementing Regulations specify 72 hours
where there is risk of harm). Delays in consumer notification following a breach are a direct PDPL
violation.

## Category 4 — Limit Sharing Your Personal Information

---

### 4-1 Read the Privacy Policy

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Shop only with reputable e-commerce service providers who have a privacy policy posted. Read
  the policy and make sure it clearly explains how your data will be used and stored by the seller.
- Avoid e-commerce service providers whose policy includes misuse of personal data (e.g. giving
  consumers' personal information (without their consent) to advertisement agencies).

**Operator implication:** Posting a clear, accessible, accurate privacy policy is a **mandatory
obligation** under PDPL Art. 11 (transparency) and the E-commerce Law. The privacy policy must
explain what data is collected, the purpose of processing, retention periods, and data subject rights.
Sharing consumer personal data with advertising agencies without consent is a direct PDPL violation
(PDPL Art. 4 — lawful basis required; marketing typically requires explicit consent under PDPL
Art. 6). Auditors will check privacy policy accessibility, completeness, and accuracy against actual
data flows.

---

### 4-2 Protect Your Personal Information

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Do not give more personal information than needed when signing up for an e-commerce account
  or shopping online.
- Only share the information that is required to enable the specific e-commerce transaction and
  do not choose to save or store your personal or financial information on the apps or website.
- Avoid sharing your credit card details over non-secure communication channels such as email
  or social media messages.

**Operator implication:** Data minimisation is a **mandatory PDPL principle** (PDPL Art. 5).
Platforms must not collect personal data beyond what is strictly necessary for the transaction.
Mandatory fields on registration forms must be justified. Storing payment card data by default
without explicit consent is a PDPL violation and potentially a PCI DSS violation. Transmitting
financial data over non-encrypted channels is a critical finding under ECC-2:2024 §2-5
(Data and Information Protection) and NCS-1:2020.

---

### 4-3 Ignore Phishing Messages

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Avoid phishing messages by checking the sender's identity carefully before opening the message.
  Phishing is a message that appears to be from a legitimate sender and intends to trick the
  recipient to reveal personal information such as username, password or credit card details.

**Operator implication:** Operators must implement email authentication standards (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
to prevent spoofing of their domain in phishing attacks — an ECC-2:2024 §2-9 (Email Protection)
requirement. Operators must also conduct phishing simulation exercises and train staff on phishing
recognition per ECC-2:2024 §1-10. Consumer-facing phishing incidents that lead to credential or
payment data theft create PDPL breach notification obligations.

---

### 4-4 Limit Permissions Granted to Apps

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
- Carefully consider whether it makes sense to give mobile apps or websites permissions to access
  a variety of data and functions on your device (e.g., location, camera, address book or
  microphone).

**Operator implication:** Mobile app developers must request only the device permissions strictly
necessary for the app's declared function (PDPL data minimisation, Art. 5). Requesting excessive
permissions (e.g., location access in a text-only commerce app) is a PDPL compliance risk and
will attract consumer and regulator scrutiny. App store submission guidelines (Apple, Google) also
enforce permission justification — excessive permissions risk app removal.

## Appendix A — Terms and Definitions (verbatim from source)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Attack | Any kind of malicious activity that attempts to achieve unauthorized access, collection, disabling, prevention, destruction or sabotage of the information system resources or the information itself. |
| Backup | Files, devices, data and procedures available for use in case of failure or loss, or in case of deletion or suspension of their original copies. |
| Cybersecurity | According to the Royal Decree number 6801, dated 11/2/1439H, cybersecurity is the protection of networks, IT systems, operational technologies systems and their components of hardware and software, their services and the data they contain, from any penetration, disruption, modification, access, use or unauthorized exploitation. The concept of cybersecurity also includes information security and digital security. |
| E-commerce Service Provider | Merchant (person who is bound by commercial registration and using e-commerce) or practitioner (person who is not bound by commercial registration but using e-commerce). |
| Hyper Text Transfer Protocol Secure (HTTPS) | A protocol that uses encryption to secure web pages and data when they are transmitted over the network. It is a secure version of the Hypertext Text Transfer Protocol (HTTP). |
| Incident | A compromise through violation of cybersecurity policies, acceptable use policies, practices incident or cybersecurity controls or requirements. |
| Phishing | The attempt to obtain sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, or credit card details, often for malicious reasons and intentions, by disguising as a trustworthy organization in email, text or social media messages. |
| Privacy | Freedom from unauthorized interference or disclosure of personal information about an individual. |
| Threat | Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organizational operations or person (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), organizational assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, and/or denial of service. Also, the potential for a threat-source to successfully exploit a particular information system vulnerability. |

## Operator Obligations Quick Reference

The table below maps CGEC consumer guidance to mandatory operator obligations under KSA law.
Use this when advising e-commerce platform operators on what consumer-facing expectations create
regulatory exposure.

| CGEC Guideline | Consumer Expectation | Operator Mandatory Obligation | Regulatory Basis |
|----------------|---------------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| 1-3 Strong passwords | Password complexity | Enforce minimum password complexity at registration | ECC-2:2024 §2-2 |
| 1-4 Additional verification | MFA option available | Offer MFA; mandatory for SAMA-licensed entities | ECC-2:2024 §2-2; SAMA CSF |
| 1-5 Update applications | Platform is patched | Patch management per cadence | ECC-2:2024 §2-3 |
| 1-7 Default settings | Privacy-safe defaults | Privacy by default; no pre-checked consent | PDPL Art. 5 |
| 1-8 Security warnings | HTTPS in use | HTTPS mandatory; valid TLS certificate | ECC-2:2024 §2-3; NCS-1:2020 |
| 2-1 Review transactions | Transaction history accessible | Provide real-time transaction records; SMS alerts | E-commerce Law; SAMA rules |
| 3-1 Spam protection | No unsolicited comms | Marketing requires lawful basis / prior consent | PDPL Art. 4, Art. 6 |
| 3-2 Verified providers | Ma'aroof registration | Register and maintain Ma'aroof listing | E-commerce Law |
| 3-3 Secure websites | HTTPS required | HTTP-only payment pages = critical finding | ECC-2:2024 §2-3; NCS-1:2020 |
| 3-6 Report incidents | Incident reporting channel | Functional consumer reporting channel; PDPL breach notification ≤72h where harm risk | E-commerce Law; PDPL Art. 20 |
| 4-1 Privacy policy | Policy posted and clear | Mandatory privacy policy meeting PDPL Art. 11 | PDPL Art. 11 |
| 4-2 Data minimisation | Collect only necessary data | Data minimisation; no unnecessary mandatory fields | PDPL Art. 5 |
| 4-3 Phishing | SPF/DKIM/DMARC on domain | Email authentication mandatory | ECC-2:2024 §2-9 |
