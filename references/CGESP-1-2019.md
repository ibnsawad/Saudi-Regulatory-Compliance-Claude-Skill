## Purpose and Rationale

CGESP-1:2019 was developed by NCA after conducting a comprehensive study of national and
international cybersecurity e-commerce guidelines, related national initiatives, statistics and
regulatory requirements, cybersecurity best practices, and previous cybersecurity incidents and
attacks on e-commerce service providers.

The document helps SME and SoHo e-commerce service providers in the Kingdom understand the
cybersecurity risks they face and provides practical guidance on protecting their businesses,
systems, and data from cyber threats.

---

## Scope of Applicability

Guidelines apply to e-commerce service providers in Saudi Arabia in the following segments:

- **SME (Small and Medium Enterprises):** Companies with 6 to 249 employees and SAR 3m to
  SAR 200m in revenues.
- **SoHo (Small Office / Home Office):** Individual sellers / companies with 1 to 5 employees
  and less than SAR 3m in revenues.

The guidelines cover conducting e-commerce using any channel (social media, websites, apps) and
any computing device (personal computers, tablets, smartphones, TVs). These are **awareness
guidelines**; NCA strongly encourages every KSA e-commerce service provider to implement them
and to conduct their own research for additional measures as the threat landscape evolves.

## Relationship to Other National Cybersecurity Publications

- **CGEC-1:2019** — Companion document targeting e-commerce consumers (not service providers).
- **ECC-1:2018 / ECC-2:2024** — Large enterprises (another segment of e-commerce service
  providers) must refer to ECC for guidance and, in some cases, mandatory compliance.
- **E-Commerce Law** — Ministry of Commerce and Investment; CGESP complements this law.
- **PDPL** — NCA Data Protection Act (noted as under development at time of CGESP issue;
  now enacted as PDPL 2021). CGESP's data protection guidance aligns with PDPL obligations.
- **SAMA Cybersecurity Framework and Banking Consumer Protection Principles** — SAMA-licensed
  entities must satisfy SAMA CSF in addition to CGESP guidance.

> **Compliance advisor note:** CGESP is non-mandatory awareness guidance for SMEs and SoHos.
> However, the practices it describes directly correspond to mandatory obligations under PDPL,
> the E-commerce Law, ECC-2:2024 (for larger entities), and SAMA CSF (for SAMA-licensed entities).
> When advising an e-commerce operator, use CGESP as a minimum baseline checklist and layer the
> mandatory frameworks on top for the entity's actual compliance obligations.

## KSA SME/SoHo E-commerce Context (as stated in CGESP source)

- Saudi Arabia's e-commerce spending estimated at **SAR 26.8 billion in 2019** — one of the
  largest e-commerce markets in MENA (Statista eCommerce Report, 2019).
- **26% of SMEs** in KSA used social media to promote products (CITC, 2017).
- ~**86% of SMEs** selling online had been active online sellers for 3–5 years at time of issue.
- Most SME providers focus on the KSA market; a few target GCC and broader Middle East.
- Almost all SoHo providers generate sales primarily through social media platforms.
- **Common threats:** e-commerce system and data compromise, denial of service, click fraud.
- **Common impacts:** revenue decrease, inventory loss, IT/website unavailability, reputational
  harm, legal consequences.

## Category 1 — Use Strong Authentication

---

### 1-1 Avoid using predictable, shared or old passwords
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
In order to protect your e-commerce accounts as a service provider, use strong passwords that are:
- Made up of random sequence of letters (both upper case and lower case), numbers, and special
  characters (!@#$%^&*).
- Not common words, simple sequence of numbers (e.g., "123456") or any personal information,
  such as your birthday or child's name, as these are easy to guess.
- Long (at least 8 characters long), which makes it harder for attackers to break it.

Make sure to change passwords at least every 3 months and never disclose passwords to others.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-2 (Identity and Access Management) mandates password complexity and rotation
policies. For SAMA-licensed entities, the SAMA CSF imposes equivalent requirements. Under PDPL,
failure to implement adequate authentication controls contributing to a data breach creates
liability for the data controller.

---

### 1-2 Change all default passwords
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Some systems and devices come with default admin passwords. Change these immediately upon
installation and before use. Default passwords are known by attackers and can be used to attack
and access your e-commerce systems. Use a different password for each one of your e-commerce
systems, applications and accounts.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System and Processing Facilities Protection) and OSMACC-1:2021
§2-3-1-3 both require hardening of default configurations including passwords. Unchanged default
credentials are a CRITICAL audit finding under both frameworks.

---

### 1-3 Consider using multi-factor authentication
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
For systems or applications that handle consumer login, consider deploying a multi-factor
authentication feature to protect your consumers' accounts. For example, when a consumer logs in
with a password, they may be sent a code (via text message on their registered mobile phone) which
they have to enter to complete the login process. Also, consider protecting your own e-commerce
account by signing up for additional authentication mechanisms (such as email messages) offered by
e-commerce apps or websites (including social media accounts).

**Regulatory mapping:**
SAMA-licensed entities: MFA is mandatory under SAMA CSF. Government/CNI entities using social
media: MFA on social media accounts is mandatory under OSMACC-1:2021 §2-2-1-5 (CRITICAL control).
For SME/SoHo: CGESP frames MFA as "consider" — but given the threat landscape, NCA and SAMA
guidance trends toward treating MFA as a baseline expectation.

## Category 2 — Protect Your E-commerce Systems

---

### 2-1 Know your e-commerce technology assets
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Keep an up to date list of all the current IT equipment, software, data and any other technology
assets you use for e-commerce. The first step in protecting your e-commerce systems is knowing
what devices and data are critical to your business. Usually, these are the assets that your
e-commerce business cannot function without.

**Regulatory mapping:**
Asset inventory is the foundation control of ECC-2:2024 §2-1 (Asset Management). For larger
entities, this is mandatory. For SMEs, maintaining an accurate asset inventory is the prerequisite
for every subsequent security control.

---

### 2-2 Control number of admin accounts
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Grant your e-commerce business employees the lowest level of user rights required to perform their
job duties. An admin account is a high privilege user account that has more power to make changes
in the system than other user accounts. Admin privileges may be given very carefully, and every
access to the admin account should be strictly controlled through a more stringent
password/multi-factor authentication and a shorter timeout period. You should also review users'
access periodically and limit the number of users who have remote access to your systems.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-2 (Identity and Access Management) — least-privilege and periodic access review
are mandatory controls. SAMA CSF requires formal privileged access management. Under PDPL, granting
excessive access to personal data is a data minimisation violation (PDPL Art. 5).

---

### 2-3 Use anti-malware software
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
To protect your e-commerce systems from malware (e.g., viruses), use and regularly update
anti-malware software on every device in your e-commerce business ecosystem (including computers,
smart phones, and tablets). It is recommended to set the anti-malware software to automatically
check for updates at least daily, and set to run a complete scan on a regular basis.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System and Processing Facilities Protection) requires endpoint
protection including anti-malware on all devices. Daily automatic update cadence reflects the
ECC vulnerability management posture.

---

### 2-4 Regularly update your applications on all devices
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Keep all your e-commerce devices and applications (especially operating systems) up to date with
security patches and vendor upgrades. This is one of the most effective ways to protect against
malware and viruses.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3 requires patch management. OSMACC-1:2021 §2-3-1-1 mandates monthly patching
for social media applications. For general e-commerce systems, patching cadence should be risk-based
with critical patches applied as soon as practicable.

---

### 2-5 Stay up to date with cybersecurity threats
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Subscribe to vendor notifications and cybersecurity alerts to keep up to date on cybersecurity
trends and active threats. You may also follow latest updates from trusted organizations (e.g.,
Computer Emergency Response Team for cybersecurity – Saudi Arabia (CERT-SA)) to learn more about
cybersecurity trends and publications.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-14 (Cybersecurity Threat Intelligence) for larger entities. For SMEs, subscribing
to CERT-SA alerts is the minimum baseline. NCA's CSCC-1:2019 (Cloud Service Consumer Controls)
similarly emphasises threat intelligence awareness.

---

### 2-6 Avoid connecting to non-secure wi-fi networks
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Avoid using non-secure networks (e.g., public Wi-Fi) for any business transaction. These networks
are an ideal location for attackers to intercept data transmission and obtain your data such as
login details and financial information.

**Regulatory mapping:**
OSMACC-1:2021 §1-4-1-6 (awareness control) explicitly requires training on avoiding untrusted
public networks. ECC-2:2024 §2-6 (Mobile Device Security) and §2-4 (Network Security Management)
address network-level controls. For SAMA-licensed entities, use of unsecured networks for financial
transactions is a direct CSF violation.

---

### 2-7 Use encryption on your website
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Consider using an encryption protocol to secure your e-commerce website. Such protocols are a good
way of ensuring that transactions conducted on your e-commerce website are secure. This is done by
encrypting any data that gets inputted to your website.

**Regulatory mapping:**
HTTPS/TLS is a **mandatory** requirement, not optional, under ECC-2:2024 §2-3, NCS-1:2020
(MODERATE baseline requires TLS 1.2+), and the E-commerce Law's secure transaction obligation.
CGESP frames this as "consider" reflecting its SME/SoHo audience, but for any entity processing
payment card data, encryption is mandatory under PCI DSS. HTTP-only payment pages are a CRITICAL
finding under any regulatory review.

---

### 2-8 Use trusted websites to display your ads
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Protect against fraudulent clicks on your ads by running your ads on trusted websites which you
know are more likely to have actual customers.

**Regulatory mapping:**
Click fraud causes direct financial harm and potentially consumer data exposure if malicious ad
redirects lead to fake sites. Operators should vet advertising networks and platforms. Malvertising
through operator-placed ads may expose the operator to reputational and legal risk under the
E-commerce Law.

## Category 3 — Minimize Impact of Data Breaches

---

### 3-1 Back up your data
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Consider backing up your critical data, which may include your business, social media and customer
information. You can use external online backup services (e.g., cloud backup) or external storage
media (e.g., external hard drive). If you choose to use a cloud service for backup, **refrain from
storing your Saudi customers' data in the cloud if that provider operates outside the Kingdom**,
in order to comply with related and applicable national cybersecurity and data protection laws and
regulations. Furthermore, perform backups regularly, at least on a weekly basis and check backup
tools effectiveness by validating data on these backups to avoid data loss.

**Regulatory mapping:**
- **Data localisation:** The instruction to avoid storing Saudi customer data with cloud providers
  outside KSA directly reflects PDPL cross-border transfer restrictions (PDPL Art. 29–30 and the
  Personal Data Transfer Regulation). Cross-border transfer requires SDAIA approval or a qualifying
  transfer mechanism. This is a **mandatory legal obligation**, not merely guidance.
- **Backup cadence:** Weekly minimum. ECC-2:2024 §2-10 (Backup and Recovery Management) requires
  regular backups with tested recovery. For CNI/government entities, backup frequency must align
  with business continuity RTOs.
- **Backup validation:** Untested backups are a finding under ECC-2:2024 §2-10.

---

### 3-2 Protect your data with encryption
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Encrypt all the critical data stored on your computer, cloud or external drive, and data sent over
email. Encrypt sensitive files on portable storage (e.g., USB flash drive) so that data cannot be
accessed by others in case you lost the portable storage.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-8 (Cryptography) mandates encryption of sensitive data at rest and in transit.
NCS-1:2020 specifies approved cryptographic standards (AES-256 for data at rest; TLS 1.2+ for
data in transit). Under PDPL, unencrypted personal data on lost portable storage constitutes a
reportable personal data breach (PDPL Art. 20).

---

### 3-3 Protect customers' financial data
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Follow data minimization principles to reduce the amount and type of consumers' data you collect
and keep. If you choose to store your customer's payment data (e.g., credit card details) on your
e-commerce platform, make sure to apply strict cybersecurity controls (e.g., limited access,
encryption) on such data to protect your business against a payment data breach. Also make sure
to comply with any relevant national or international cybersecurity laws and regulations. For
example, if you have customers outside Saudi Arabia, make sure to learn about any relevant
mandatory requirements that your business must comply with (e.g., GDPR for EU customers). Make
sure you report to SAMA and your customers any data breaches affecting your customers' payment
data.

**Regulatory mapping:**
- **Data minimisation:** Mandatory under PDPL Art. 5.
- **Payment card data storage:** Any entity storing, processing, or transmitting cardholder data
  must comply with PCI DSS — a contractual requirement imposed by payment brands, not merely a
  recommendation.
- **Breach notification to SAMA:** Entities licensed by SAMA must report payment data breaches per
  SAMA CSF incident reporting obligations. For all entities under PDPL, personal data breaches that
  may cause harm require notification to SDAIA (Art. 20, Implementing Regulations: within 72 hours
  where harm risk exists) and notification to affected data subjects without undue delay.
- **Cross-border applicability:** GDPR applies when serving EU data subjects — a direct legal
  obligation for operators with European customers regardless of where the business is headquartered.

---

### 3-4 Change your default security settings
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Review and change the default preferences in your web browser and e-commerce apps to avoid saving
social media login credentials, customer information and autofill retrieval. If you do online
banking for your business, consider having a dedicated device which is used only for online banking
and make sure to disconnect it from the network when it is not in use.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3-1-3 / OSMACC-1:2021 §2-3-1-3 require hardening of default configurations.
PDPL privacy-by-default principle (Art. 5) requires that default settings protect personal data.
Dedicated banking devices reflect ECC-2:2024 network segmentation and device security controls.

---

### 3-5 Enable remote wiping on your mobile devices
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Mobile devices normally come with a feature for remotely wiping all content in case the user lost
his/her device. Activate that feature and ask employees to promptly report any missing business
mobile device as soon as possible so that the device can be recovered, locked out or remotely
wiped. This will prevent attackers from accessing your e-commerce systems and accounts.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-6 (Mobile Device Security) requires MDM and remote wipe capability for
organisational mobile devices. OSMACC-1:2021 §2-4-1-1 mandates MDM for devices used for social
media account management. Failure to remote-wipe a lost device containing personal data is a
potential PDPL breach contributing factor.

## Category 4 — Guard Your Social Media Accounts Used in E-commerce

---

### 4-1 Exercise safe behavior on social media
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Take extra care while using social networking apps and websites. When communicating through social
media, be suspicious of any messages that ask for sensitive business information. Social networking
sites are becoming an increasingly popular way for attackers to try to get your business
information. Assign someone to be mainly responsible for your e-commerce social media accounts.

**Regulatory mapping:**
OSMACC-1:2021 §1-3 (Cybersecurity in Human Resources) requires designating responsible personnel
for social media accounts and ensuring they receive specific cybersecurity awareness. For
government/CNI entities, OSMACC compliance (including designated accountability) is mandatory.

---

### 4-2 Get your social media accounts verified
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Consider getting your social media account verified. Most social media platforms offer a visible
indication (e.g., icon next to profile username) to show that this account is verified. Getting
verified is a way to increase the customers' confidence in this account. Online review platforms
(such as Ma'aroof — https://maroof.sa) are a good way to get verified as well. Be careful of
connection requests from other businesses you do not know. You may do a quick online search of the
entity to make sure they are legitimate. Avoid linking your social media account to unknown
services or apps, and revoke any unnecessary access.

**Regulatory mapping:**
OSMACC-1:2021 §2-2-1-3 mandates account verification for government/CNI entities. Ma'aroof
registration is required under the E-commerce Law for merchants. Unnecessary linked third-party
apps on social media accounts are an access management control gap under OSMACC §2-2-1-8 and
ECC-2:2024 §2-2.

## Category 5 — Defend Your Network

---

### 5-1 Disable unnecessary services on systems
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Many devices such as computers and wireless routers come with extra services and features which are
often not used. Similarly, many operating systems and apps have extra and unnecessary services and
software programs pre-installed. Disable or turn off these features and services.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System Protection) requires hardening of systems including disabling
unnecessary services. Attack surface reduction is a foundational security principle. OSMACC-1:2021
§2-3-1-4 requires restricting features and services in social media accounts on a need basis.

---

### 5-2 Segment and segregate networks
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Divide your network into sub-networks to protect sensitive information from flowing within the
non-secure parts of your e-commerce network.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-4 (Network Security Management) mandates network segmentation and segregation.
For OTCC-1:2022 environments (OT/IT convergence), network segmentation is a CRITICAL control.
For e-commerce platforms handling payment data, network segmentation is a PCI DSS Requirement 1
control (isolating the Cardholder Data Environment).

---

### 5-3 Defend network perimeter
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Use Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) in order to defend your network perimeter. These systems
can detect unusual data traffic patterns or anomalies in network activities which may indicate that
someone is trying to attack your systems. Another recommended way to defend your network is to
implement a firewall in your network. Make sure to review the access list periodically.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-4 (Network Security Management) mandates firewall/IPS deployment and periodic
access list review. SAMA CSF requires IPS/IDS for financial service entities. Periodic access list
review is a specific calendar obligation — undated or stale access control lists are a finding.

---

### 5-4 Test your systems regularly
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Conduct penetration tests on your e-commerce systems regularly and anytime you have a major code
change or a system upgrade. Penetration testing simulates a cyber-attack such as a DDoS to identify
weaknesses in the tested system.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-11 (Penetration Testing) mandates periodic penetration testing for NCA-scoped
entities. PCI DSS Requirement 11 mandates annual penetration testing and testing after significant
changes. For SAMA-licensed entities, penetration testing scope and frequency are defined by the
SAMA CSF. Testing after major code changes is a specific trigger — failure to test a significant
deployment is a finding.

## Category 6 — Continuously Educate and Train Your Employees

---

### 6-1 Develop and implement cybersecurity and privacy policies
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Develop and implement a cybersecurity policy that defines what employees can and cannot do when
using business systems. The policy should highlight the consequences that employees will face in
case of violating this policy. Consider training your employees periodically on the cybersecurity
requirements highlighted in your cybersecurity policy. Also, consider developing a privacy policy
that is based on internationally accepted privacy principles. Make sure to publish a privacy notice
that refers to this policy on your e-commerce store to communicate to consumers your commitment to
protecting their privacy and their personal data.

**Regulatory mapping:**
- **Cybersecurity policy:** Mandatory under ECC-2:2024 §1-3 (Cybersecurity Policies and
  Procedures) for NCA-scoped entities.
- **Privacy policy/notice:** **Mandatory** under PDPL Art. 11 — a privacy policy must be published
  and must accurately describe data processing activities. Absence of a privacy notice is a direct
  PDPL violation. For government entities, NDMO-Standards §3 (Data Governance) and PDPL apply
  simultaneously.
- **Employee training on policy:** ECC-2:2024 §1-10 (Cybersecurity Awareness and Training Program)
  mandates periodic training.

---

### 6-2 Protect against phishing and social engineering
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Educate your employees and protect against phishing and social engineering. Social engineering
refers to psychological manipulation of people into revealing confidential personal and financial
information. Attackers usually use phishing emails to conduct social engineering attacks and gather
the information they need to commit fraud or gain access to business computer systems or social
media accounts. You should educate your employees on the common signs of phishing emails that
include, for example:
- Poor spelling, grammar and punctuation.
- Instead of addressing a specific person, they refer to 'friend' or 'valued colleague'.
- Sense of urgency, in terms of time or request from a senior person in the company.
- May offer a great discount on a popular item.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-9 (Email Protection) requires email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) to reduce
phishing exposure. ECC-2:2024 §1-10 mandates phishing-awareness training. For SAMA-licensed
entities, anti-phishing controls and social engineering awareness are mandatory CSF requirements.
Successful phishing attacks that lead to data breaches trigger PDPL breach notification obligations.

---

### 6-3 Restrict staff from downloading unknown applications
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Unknown applications are one of the most likely sources of malware. Train employees to only use
software that is necessary for business and to download that from trusted sources (e.g., app
stores, vendor's official website).

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-3 (Information System Protection) — application allowlisting and restricting
unapproved software installation. ECC-2:2024 §1-10 — awareness training on safe software use.
MDM systems (required for mobile devices under OSMACC §2-4-1-1 for government/CNI entities) can
enforce application restrictions technically.

---

### 6-4 Recognize signs of compromise and how to handle incidents
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Train employees to handle incidents and recognize signs of compromise. Also train employees on
reporting cybersecurity incidents immediately. Typical signs may include:
- Computer, applications or network will respond very slowly to commands and requests.
- Locked out of key accounts and receiving messages that your password has been changed when you
  did not change it.
- Unable to access your files, applications or services.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-13 (Cybersecurity Incident and Threat Management) mandates incident response
procedures and staff training on incident recognition. OSMACC-1:2021 §2-7-1-1 requires a
documented recovery plan for social media account incidents. Under PDPL Art. 20, incidents involving
personal data must be reported to SDAIA promptly; delays due to staff failing to recognise a
compromise or report it immediately are a direct compliance failure.

## Category 7 — Strengthen Your Internal E-commerce Infrastructure

---

### 7-1 Keep your backup in a secure location
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
If you are using external hard drives for backup, keep these external backup media in a secure
location, either in a small fire-resistant safe or preferably in a different building.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-10 (Backup and Recovery Management) requires secure storage of backup media,
including offsite storage for critical backups. Physical security of backup media is a control that
maps to ECC-2:2024 §2-15 (Physical Security). Backup stored in the same physical location as
primary systems is a single point of failure — a finding under any BCP/DR assessment.

---

### 7-2 Implement a spam filter
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Implement a spam filter on your email exchange. A spam filter will block most spam and only allow
legitimate and acceptable emails to get to your e-commerce business. Refrain from opening links in
emails sent by your consumers as these may lead to malicious websites or applications. Also, keep
your employees' emails addresses confidential and use generic email addresses (e.g.,
help@companyname.com) for information posted publicly.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-9 (Email Protection) mandates email security controls including anti-spam. Using
personal employee email addresses in public-facing communications is a data exposure risk and
contradicts PDPL Art. 5 data minimisation (exposing individual employee identities unnecessarily).
Using generic role-based addresses reduces social engineering risk.

---

### 7-3 Review audit trails and security logs
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Continuously monitor and frequently review audit trails and security logs. Audit trails and
security event logs (e.g., logs that contain records of login/logout activity) aid in the detection
and investigation of cyberattacks. The audit trails will show who accessed the IT systems and what
operations were performed. Maintaining proper audit trails and security logs will also help in
recovering lost transactions, highlighting security weaknesses and potential points of illegal
intrusion for your e-commerce business.

**Regulatory mapping:**
ECC-2:2024 §2-12 (Cybersecurity Event Logs and Monitoring Management) mandates log collection,
retention, and review. SAMA CSF requires continuous monitoring for SAMA-licensed entities. Log
retention periods must comply with relevant regulations — under PDPL, logs containing personal
data (e.g., user login records) are themselves personal data subject to retention limitation
principles (PDPL Art. 5).

---

### 7-4 Use email activation and captcha for user registration
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
To avoid mass registrations done by spam campaigns, require users to confirm registrations through
clicking a confirmation link in their email or using a CAPTCHA field (online test used to determine
whether or not the user is human). The key is to make the confirmation link process easy to follow
(email could arrive promptly, setting a timeout, etc.) and the CAPTCHA challenge simple enough for
legitimate users not to get frustrated.

**Regulatory mapping:**
Bot-driven mass registration is both a fraud vector and a data quality issue under PDPL (fake
accounts contain fabricated data that should not be retained). CAPTCHA and email verification are
standard fraud prevention controls. For SAMA-licensed entities, preventing automated fraud at
account creation is a CSF consumer protection requirement.

---

### 7-5 Utilize fraud prevention software
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Utilize an e-commerce anti-fraud software solution as this is considered a good way to stop attacks
such as click fraud, mass registration and inventory abuse. Several vendors offer solutions that
combine a click fraud or mass registration detection algorithm with a blacklist of known fraudulent
IP addresses.

**Regulatory mapping:**
For SAMA-licensed payment entities, fraud detection systems are a mandatory CSF requirement.
For SME e-commerce operators, anti-fraud tooling is strongly recommended and the absence of any
fraud detection capability is a risk management gap that regulators and auditors will note under
the E-commerce Law's operator responsibility provisions.

---

### 7-6 Choose a secure e-commerce platform
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
When looking for the right e-commerce website or business payments platform, choose trusted and
reputable companies that have good reviews and are transparent about their privacy policy. Look for
international vendors which comply with ISO or PCI standards or local vendors who adhere to
cybersecurity standards issued by local regulators (e.g., NCA, SAMA). Similarly, secure payment
gateways come with security features such as payment authentication systems (e.g., Verified by
Visa) and fraud profiling services, which can check IP addresses, names and previous purchases, to
understand if a purchase is legitimate. You should also proactively review the breach notification
requirements and liability protections provided with the payment gateway in case of cyberattacks.

**Regulatory mapping:**
- **Third-party platform selection:** ECC-2:2024 §4-1 (Third-Party Cybersecurity) requires due
  diligence on third-party platforms including cybersecurity assessment.
- **Payment gateway compliance:** PCI DSS compliance is mandatory for payment gateway providers.
  Operators must ensure their chosen gateway is PCI DSS certified.
- **Breach notification liability:** Operators must understand contractual breach notification
  obligations with payment providers and ensure these align with PDPL Art. 20 timelines.
- **Local regulatory compliance:** SAMA-licensed payment entities must be certified against SAMA
  CSF. NCA-issued standards (ECC, OSMACC, etc.) apply to government/CNI entities.

### 7-7 Create a customized user registration
**Applicability:** SME only

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
If you are going to allow users to register on your website, create a customized form and link.
Malicious bots (a software application that runs automated tasks over the Internet) are generally
programmed to go looking for default links or input parameters to register fake users. Many
e-commerce platforms offer customization feature to avoid being detected by bots.

**Regulatory mapping:**
Custom registration flows complement CAPTCHA (7-4) in reducing bot-driven fraud. Default
registration endpoints are a known attack surface — leaving them unchanged is a configuration
hardening failure analogous to ECC-2:2024 §2-3-1-3 (hardening default configurations).

### 7-8 Protect against denial of inventory
**Applicability:** SoHo + SME

**Guideline text (verbatim from source):**
Denial of inventory is a serious problem in which bots can hold items from a limited inventory,
but never actually purchase them, thus denying the goods to other legitimate consumers. Reduce the
threat of denial of inventory by, for example, applying shopping cart policies, where you limit the
absolute time during which users can hold items or restricting the amount of times they can add the
item back to cart.

**Regulatory mapping:**
Denial of inventory is both a business continuity risk and, when consumer-facing availability is
impacted, a potential E-commerce Law consumer-protection issue. Shopping cart timeout policies are
a standard platform-level control. For large platforms, anti-bot controls (7-5) and rate limiting
(network perimeter, 5-3) together address this threat.

## Appendix A — Terms and Definitions (verbatim from source)

| CAPTCHA | Acronym for Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart — a computer program or system intended to distinguish human from machine input, typically as a way of thwarting spam and automated extraction of data from websites. |

| Threat | Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organizational operations or person (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), organizational assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, and/or denial of service.
