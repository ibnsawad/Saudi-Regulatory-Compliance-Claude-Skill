## Purpose

CGESP-1:2019 was developed by NCA after conducting a comprehensive study of national and international cybersecurity e-commerce guidelines, related national initiatives, statistics and regulatory requirements, cybersecurity best practices, and previous cybersecurity incidents and attacks on e-commerce service providers.

The document is designed to help SME and SoHo e-commerce service providers in KSA understand the cybersecurity risks they face and provides practical guidance on protecting their businesses, systems, and data from cyber threats. It is distinct from CGEC-1:2019, which targets consumers, and from ECC-1:2018, which targets large enterprises under mandatory compliance obligations.

---

## Context: KSA SME/SoHo e-commerce landscape (as stated in CGESP)

- Saudi Arabia's e-commerce spending was estimated at SAR 26.8 billion in 2019, making the country one of the largest e-commerce markets in MENA (Statista eCommerce Report, 2019).
- 26% of SMEs in the Kingdom used social media to promote their products (CITC study, 2017).
- Approximately 86% of SMEs selling online had been active as online sellers for three to five years at the time of publication.
- Most SME e-commerce providers focused on the Saudi Arabian market; a few also targeted GCC and broader Middle East markets.
- Almost all SoHo e-commerce providers generated sales primarily through social media platforms.
- Common threats include e-commerce system and data compromise, denial of service, and click fraud, leading to revenue decreases, inventory loss, system unavailability, reputational harm, and legal consequences.

## Scope of applicability

CGESP applies to e-commerce service providers in Saudi Arabia in two segments (as defined by Monsha'at — Small and Medium Enterprises General Authority):

| Segment | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **SoHo** (Small Office / Home Office) | Individual sellers / companies with 1–5 employees and less than SAR 3 million in revenues |
| **SME** (Small and Medium Enterprises) | Companies with 6–249 employees and SAR 3 million to SAR 200 million in revenues |

Coverage: any channel (social media, websites, apps) on any computing device (personal computers, tablets, smartphones, TVs).

> **Note for compliance advisors:** Large enterprises (outside the SME/SoHo definition) should refer to ECC-1:2018 (and now ECC-2:2024) rather than CGESP. CGESP is awareness guidance — there is no formal compliance penalty for CGESP non-compliance alone, but obligations in PDPL, ECC, the E-Commerce Act, and SAMA frameworks all apply independently.

## Key guidelines per category — obligations table

### Category 1 — Use Strong Authentication

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 1-1 | Avoid predictable, shared or old passwords | SoHo + SME | Passwords must use random sequences of upper/lower case letters, numbers, and special characters (!@#$%^&*); avoid common words, sequential numbers, or personal information; minimum 8 characters; rotate at least every 3 months; never share passwords |
| 1-2 | Change all default passwords | SoHo + SME | Change default admin passwords on all systems and devices immediately upon installation and before use; use a different password for each e-commerce system, application, and account |
| 1-3 | Consider using MFA | SoHo + SME | Deploy MFA on consumer-facing login systems (e.g., OTP via SMS on registered mobile); enable additional authentication for own e-commerce accounts on platforms and social media |

### Category 2 — Protect Your E-commerce Systems

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 2-1 | Know your technology assets | SoHo + SME | Maintain an up-to-date inventory of all IT equipment, software, data, and technology assets used for e-commerce; identify which are critical to business operations |
| 2-2 | Control admin accounts | SME only | Apply least-privilege access; grant admin privileges only when necessary; enforce stronger passwords/MFA and shorter timeout periods on admin accounts; review user access periodically; limit remote-access accounts |
| 2-3 | Use anti-malware software | SoHo + SME | Install anti-malware on every device in the e-commerce ecosystem (computers, smartphones, tablets); set to auto-update at least daily; run complete scans regularly |
| 2-4 | Regularly update applications | SoHo + SME | Keep all devices and applications (especially operating systems) current with security patches and vendor upgrades — one of the most effective defences against malware |
| 2-5 | Stay up to date with threats | SoHo + SME | Subscribe to vendor notifications and cybersecurity alerts; follow updates from trusted sources such as CERT-SA |
| 2-6 | Avoid non-secure Wi-Fi | SoHo + SME | Never use public or non-secure Wi-Fi networks for any business transaction; attackers can intercept login credentials and financial information on such networks |
| 2-7 | Use encryption on your website | SoHo + SME | Implement an encryption protocol (e.g., HTTPS/TLS) on your e-commerce website to secure all data inputted by users during transactions |
| 2-8 | Use trusted websites for ads | SoHo + SME | Run advertising only on trusted, reputable websites to protect against fraudulent ad clicks (click fraud) |

### Category 3 — Minimize Impact of Data Breaches

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 3-1 | Back up your data | SoHo + SME | Back up business, social media, and customer data regularly — at minimum weekly; validate backup effectiveness; if using cloud backup, do not store Saudi customer data with a cloud provider operating outside the Kingdom (data localisation obligation under national cybersecurity and data protection laws) |
| 3-2 | Protect data with encryption | SoHo + SME | Encrypt all critical data at rest (computer, cloud, external drive) and in transit (email); encrypt sensitive files on portable storage (USB drives) to prevent unauthorised access if lost |
| 3-3 | Protect customers' financial data | SoHo + SME | Apply data minimisation to consumer data collection; if storing payment data (e.g., credit card details), apply strict cybersecurity controls (limited access, encryption); comply with applicable national and international regulations (e.g., PDPL for KSA customers, GDPR for EU customers); report payment data breaches to SAMA and affected customers |
| 3-4 | Change default security settings | SoHo + SME | Review and change default browser and app preferences to prevent saving social media credentials, customer information, and autofill data; if conducting online banking for the business, use a dedicated device disconnected from the network when not in use |
| 3-5 | Enable remote wiping on mobile devices | SoHo + SME | Activate remote wipe features on all business mobile devices; require employees to promptly report any missing device to enable timely recovery, lock-out, or remote wipe |

### Category 4 — Guard Your Social Media Accounts Used in E-commerce

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 4-1 | Exercise safe behavior on social media | SoHo + SME | Exercise caution when communicating via social media; be suspicious of messages requesting sensitive business information; assign a specific person as primarily responsible for e-commerce social media accounts |
| 4-2 | Get social media accounts verified | SoHo + SME | Seek platform verification (e.g., verified icon on profile); register on Ma'aroof (maroof.sa) to increase consumer confidence; be cautious of unknown connection requests; verify legitimacy of other businesses before connecting; avoid linking social media accounts to unknown services or apps; revoke unnecessary access |

### Category 5 — Defend Your Network

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 5-1 | Disable unnecessary services | SoHo + SME | Disable or turn off all unused features, services, and pre-installed software on computers, routers, operating systems, and applications to reduce attack surface |
| 5-2 | Segment and segregate networks | SME only | Divide the network into sub-networks to prevent sensitive information from flowing through non-secure parts of the e-commerce network |
| 5-3 | Defend network perimeter | SME only | Deploy Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) to detect unusual traffic patterns and anomalies; implement a firewall; review access lists periodically |
| 5-4 | Test your systems regularly | SME only | Conduct penetration tests regularly and after every major code change or system upgrade; penetration testing simulates attacks (e.g., DDoS) to identify weaknesses |

### Category 6 — Continuously Educate and Train Your Employees

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 6-1 | Develop and implement cybersecurity and privacy policies | SME only | Create and implement a cybersecurity policy defining acceptable use and consequences of violations; train employees periodically on the policy; develop a privacy policy based on internationally accepted privacy principles; publish a privacy notice on the e-commerce store |
| 6-2 | Protect against phishing and social engineering | SME only | Educate employees on phishing and social engineering; train staff to recognise phishing indicators: poor spelling/grammar, generic greetings, urgency/authority pressure, suspicious discounts; social engineering targets business credentials and account access |
| 6-3 | Restrict staff from downloading unknown applications | SME only | Train employees to use only business-necessary software downloaded exclusively from trusted sources (official app stores, vendor websites) — unknown apps are a primary malware vector |
| 6-4 | Recognise signs of compromise and handle incidents | SoHo + SME | Train employees to identify compromise indicators: slow system/network responses, unexpected account lockouts or password-change notices, inability to access files or services; train staff to report cybersecurity incidents immediately |

### Category 7 — Strengthen Your Internal E-commerce Infrastructure

| Ref | Guideline | Scope | Key Obligations / Recommended Practices |
|-----|-----------|-------|----------------------------------------|
| 7-1 | Keep backup in a secure location | SME only | Store external hard drive backups in a physically secure location — at minimum a fire-resistant safe, preferably in a separate building |
| 7-2 | Implement a spam filter | SoHo + SME | Implement a spam filter on email exchange to block unwanted messages; avoid opening links in emails from consumers (may lead to malicious sites); keep employee email addresses confidential; use generic addresses (e.g., help@companyname.com) for publicly posted contact information |
| 7-3 | Review audit trails and security logs | SME only | Continuously monitor and frequently review audit trails and security event logs (e.g., login/logout activity); logs support detection and investigation of attacks, transaction recovery, identification of security weaknesses, and detection of intrusion points |
| 7-4 | Use email activation and CAPTCHA for user registration | SME only | Require email confirmation (via confirmation link) and/or CAPTCHA at user registration to prevent mass bot registrations; keep the process user-friendly (prompt email delivery, reasonable timeout) |
| 7-5 | Utilize fraud prevention software | SME only | Deploy e-commerce anti-fraud software to counter click fraud, mass registration, and inventory abuse; solutions combining fraud-detection algorithms with blacklists of known fraudulent IP addresses are recommended |
| 7-6 | Choose a secure e-commerce platform | SoHo + SME | Select trusted and reputable platforms with good reviews and transparent privacy policies; prefer vendors complying with ISO or PCI standards (international) or NCA/SAMA cybersecurity standards (local); secure payment gateways should offer payment authentication (e.g., Verified by Visa) and fraud profiling; proactively review breach notification and liability provisions |
| 7-7 | Create a customised user registration | SME only | Customise registration forms and URLs to avoid default parameters that malicious bots target for fake user registration; many e-commerce platforms offer built-in customisation features for this purpose |
| 7-8 | Protect against denial of inventory | SoHo + SME | Implement shopping cart policies to limit the time users can hold items in cart and restrict repeated add-to-cart actions; these prevent bots from monopolising limited inventory without purchasing |

## Common pitfalls

1. **Treating CGESP as a substitute for ECC.** CGESP is targeted at SMEs and SoHos and is non-mandatory awareness guidance. Any NCA-scoped entity — including mid-size e-commerce firms — still has mandatory ECC-2:2024 obligations. Growing businesses must reassess their framework obligations as they scale past SoHo/SME definitions.

2. **Missing the data-localisation warning in §3-1.** CGESP explicitly states that Saudi customer data should not be stored with cloud providers operating outside the Kingdom. This aligns with NDMO data standards and the PDPL Transfer Regulation. Advisors must raise this when reviewing cloud backup arrangements.

3. **Ignoring the SAMA breach-reporting obligation in §3-3.** CGESP instructs e-commerce providers to report payment data breaches to SAMA and to customers. This is a direct regulatory obligation under SAMA rules, not just advisory guidance.

4. **Conflating Ma'aroof registration with full legal compliance.** CGESP §4-2 recommends Ma'aroof (maroof.sa) registration for seller verification. Ma'aroof registration demonstrates seller legitimacy and consumer trust but does not itself satisfy cybersecurity controls obligations under ECC, PDPL, or the E-Commerce Act.

5. **Overlooking the SME-only scope of network and personnel controls.** Several high-value controls in Categories 5, 6, and 7 (network segmentation, penetration testing, employee policies, audit logs) are marked SME-only in the source document. SoHo operators are not expected to implement these at SME scale, but the underlying security principles still apply proportionately.

6. **No ownership model for social media accounts.** CGESP §4-1 explicitly calls for a named individual to be "mainly responsible" for e-commerce social media accounts. Absence of this assignment is a governance gap that also surfaces under OSMACC-1:2021.

7. **Selecting e-commerce platforms without reviewing breach notification terms.** CGESP §7-6 specifically advises reviewing the breach notification requirements and liability protections within payment gateway agreements. Many SMEs sign default terms without reviewing these provisions — creating regulatory and commercial exposure.

8. **Denial-of-inventory attacks overlooked.** The inventory-denial attack vector (§7-8) is KSA-specific in that it directly affects SME profitability and consumer experience. Bot-based cart-holding attacks are common in the region and are rarely addressed in standard cybersecurity frameworks, making CGESP's guidance on shopping cart policies practically valuable.

## Key definitions (from CGESP Appendix A)

| Term | CGESP Definition |
|------|----------------|
| Attack | Any kind of malicious activity attempting to achieve unauthorized access, collection, disabling, prevention, destruction, or sabotage of information system resources or the information itself |
| Backup | Files, devices, data, and procedures available for use in case of failure, loss, deletion, or suspension of their original copies |
| CAPTCHA | Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart — a program or system intended to distinguish human from machine input, typically to thwart spam and automated data extraction |
| Cybersecurity | Per Royal Decree 6801 (11/2/1439H): protection of networks, IT systems, OT systems, and their hardware, software, services, and data from penetration, disruption, modification, access, use, or unauthorized exploitation; includes information security and digital security |
| E-commerce Service Provider | Merchant (bound by commercial registration and using e-commerce) or practitioner (not bound by commercial registration but using e-commerce) |
| Incident | A compromise through violation of cybersecurity policies, acceptable use policies, practices, or cybersecurity controls or requirements |
| Malware | A program that infects systems, usually covertly, with the intent of compromising confidentiality, integrity, or availability of the victim's data, applications, or OS, or of otherwise disrupting the victim |
| MFA (Multifactor Authentication) | Security feature verifying user identity through multiple separate elements: Knowledge (something only the user knows, e.g., password), Possession (something only the user owns, e.g., OTP device/SMS), Inherent Characteristics (e.g., fingerprint) |
| Online Backup | A method of storage where backup is regularly taken on a remote server over a network (within the organisation's network or hosted by a service provider) |
| Patch | A data pack used to upgrade, fix, or improve computer operating systems, software, or applications; includes security vulnerability fixes and bug fixes |
| Privacy | Freedom from unauthorized interference or disclosure of personal information about an individual |
| SME | Companies with 6–249 employees and SAR 3 million to SAR 200 million in revenues |
| SoHo | Individual sellers / companies with 1–5 employees and less than SAR 3 million in revenues |
| Threat | Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organisational operations, assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification, or denial of service; also the potential for a threat source to successfully exploit a particular vulnerability |

## Related frameworks

| Framework | Relationship to CGESP |
|-----------|----------------------|
| ECC-2:2024 (ECC-1:2018 at time of CGESP issue) | Mandatory baseline for large enterprises and NCA-scoped entities; CGESP is the SME/SoHo equivalent guidance; many CGESP guidelines map directly to ECC controls |
| CGEC-1:2019 | Consumer-facing companion document published concurrently; consumer guidelines in CGEC are the customer-side counterpart to operator guidelines in CGESP |
| PDPL (Royal Decree M/19, 2021) | Legal basis for data protection obligations implied throughout CGESP (data minimisation §3-3, privacy policy §6-1, breach notification); PDPL was under development at the time CGESP was issued |
| SAMA Cybersecurity Framework + Consumer Protection Principles | Explicitly referenced in CGESP as complementary; governs payment data breach reporting (§3-3) and consumer payment protection |
| OSMACC-1:2021 | Extends CGESP Category 4 social media security guidance into a full mandatory controls framework for organisational social media accounts; advisors should apply OSMACC-1:2021 alongside CGESP Category 4 |
| E-Commerce Act (Ministry of Commerce and Investment) | Primary e-commerce regulation in KSA; CGESP was developed as a cybersecurity companion to this Act |
| Ma'aroof Platform (maroof.sa) | Government seller-verification registry explicitly referenced in CGESP §4-2; operated by the Ministry of Commerce; registration increases consumer confidence and supports E-Commerce Act compliance |
| PCI-DSS | Referenced in CGESP §7-6 as an international standard for payment platform selection; e-commerce providers processing card payments should assess PCI-DSS applicability independently |
| GDPR | Referenced in CGESP §3-3 as an example of an international requirement that applies to KSA e-commerce providers with EU customers |
