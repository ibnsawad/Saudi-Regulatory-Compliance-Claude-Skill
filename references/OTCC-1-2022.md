## Purpose

Provides cybersecurity controls tailored for Industrial Control Systems (ICS) to increase the protection of critical infrastructures that operate or depend on these systems. Developed by NCA following a comprehensive study of national and international cybersecurity frameworks, standards, and OT/ICS-specific guidance. Addresses the four cybersecurity pillars: Strategy, People, Process, and Technology.

> **ICS definition (from the document):** ICS includes all devices, systems, or networks used to operate and/or automate industrial processes.

---

## Applicability (Scope of Work)

Controls apply to ICS that reside in **critical facilities** owned and/or operated by:

- Government organisations in KSA (including ministries, authorities, establishments, and others), including those operating **inside or outside the Kingdom**
- Private-sector organisations **owning, operating, or hosting Critical National Infrastructures (CNIs)**

**Critical facility** is defined as a facility where destruction and/or dysfunction may lead to the disruption or discontinuity of the organisation's operation.

NCA strongly encourages all other KSA organisations to adopt the controls as best practice.

### Statement of Applicability

Every in-scope organisation that owns or operates ICS technologies must comply with all applicable controls **after ensuring that applying those controls will not jeopardise the continuity of the organisation's operation**. This safety-continuity qualifier is unique to OTCC and distinguishes it from other NCA frameworks.

## Control levels (criticality tiers)

OTCC uses **3 levels** based on facility criticality. Levels are **cumulative** (L1 includes all L2 and L3 controls; L2 includes all L3 controls). The appropriate level is determined using NCA's **Facility Level Identification Tool**.

| Level | Facility criticality | Controls included | Criteria |
|-------|---------------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Level 1 (L1)** | High | 151 controls and subcontrols (including L2 and L3) | Severe adverse effects, catastrophic consequences, and/or impacts to operations, assets, resources, or HSE |
| **Level 2 (L2)** | Moderate | 117 controls and subcontrols (including L3) | Significant effects, consequences, and/or impacts to operations, assets, resources, or HSE |
| **Level 3 (L3)** | Low | 56 controls and subcontrols | Moderate adverse effects, consequences, and/or impacts to operations, assets, resources, or HSE |

**Three criteria drive level assignment:**
1. Criticality, consequences, and impact on the organisation's business and services availability
2. Negative impact on Health, Safety, and/or Environment (HSE)
3. Negative impact on national economy, national security, or social influence

> **Practical note:** Subcontrol-level applicability by L1/L2/L3 is provided in the OTCC Methodology and Mapping Annex (a separate NCA document). The main OTCC document tables show checkmarks (✔) against each subcontrol for each applicable level — these are reproduced in the control tables below.

## Audit hot spots — triage list

Ranked by frequency in NCA OTCC gap assessments. Each item cites the source control.

1. **ECC prerequisite not in place (all domains):** OTCC compliance requires ECC-1:2018 compliance as a mandatory prerequisite. Organisations assessed for OTCC without ECC baseline in place fail at the opening question.
2. **Facility level not assigned using the Facility Level Identification Tool (1-3-1-4):** Many organisations self-assign a level without using NCA's approved tool, leading to under-scoped control sets.
3. **OT/ICS IAM not separated from IT IAM (2-2-1-1):** OT identity and access management lifecycle shared with IT systems — single AD/IAM platform governing both environments — is non-compliant at L1.
4. **Default credentials not changed on OT/ICS assets (2-2-1-3):** PLCs, HMIs, SCADA servers, and network devices shipped with factory-default credentials and never changed — one of the most exploited OT weaknesses.
5. **Remote access to OT/ICS networks not restricted and risk-assessed (2-2-1-7):** Vendor VPN tunnels or IT remote access tools connected directly into OT/ICS networks without a prior risk assessment, MFA, session monitoring, or time-limited access.
6. **OT/ICS environment not segmented from corporate/IT network (2-4-1-1, 2-4-1-9):** Direct IP routing between the corporate LAN and OT/ICS networks without a DMZ, jump host, or data diode.
7. **No OT-specific asset inventory (2-1-1-1):** Asset inventories exist for IT but OT/ICS assets (PLCs, RTUs, sensors, field devices) are not inventoried in electronic format or not maintained at all.
8. **SIS not separately segmented (2-4-1-3):** Safety Instrumented Systems on the same network as process control or corporate systems — a critical safety and cybersecurity risk.
9. **Backups not stored offline or off-site (2-8-1-1):** OT/ICS backups stored only on-site and network-connected; configuration files and engineering files not included in backup scope.
10. **Vulnerability assessments not meeting cadence (2-9-1-3):** Assessments conducted annually regardless of facility level. L1 facilities require quarterly assessments; missing the 3-month cadence is a recurring finding.
11. **Penetration testing not covering OT/ICS environment (2-10-1-1):** IT penetration tests scoped to the corporate network only; OT/ICS networks and operational technology explicitly excluded.
12. **No OT-specific incident response plan integrated with BCP (2-12-1-1):** Generic IT incident response plan cited as covering OT — but does not include SIS recovery, safe-mode operation procedures, or integration with the organisation's BCM.
13. **Application whitelisting absent in OT/ICS environment (2-3-1-6):** No application control deployed on HMIs, EWS, or OT servers — allowing arbitrary code execution.
14. **External media used without scanning (2-3-1-8):** USB drives, maintenance laptops, and configuration tablets connected to OT/ICS assets without prior malware scanning in an isolated environment.
15. **Third-party vendors given unrestricted physical or logical access (2-13-1-7, 4-1-1-1):** OEM/vendor maintenance access not monitored; no cybersecurity requirements in vendor contracts or procurement lifecycle.

## Common pitfalls

- **ECC treated as parallel rather than prerequisite:** Many organisations begin OTCC implementation without ECC compliance in place. OTCC controls explicitly extend specific ECC controls — attempting OTCC without ECC creates control gaps at the base layer.
- **IT and OT treated as a single security domain:** Shared firewalls, shared identity systems, shared patch management, and shared monitoring are common in OT environments but non-compliant at L1. OT and IT must have separated IAM, network segmentation, and change management processes.
- **Safety-continuity qualifier misused to avoid controls:** The statement that controls must not "jeopardise continuity of operation" is sometimes used as a blanket justification to avoid implementing security measures. The OTCC requires documented justification approved by the cybersecurity function and Authorizing Official when controls cannot be applied — not a blanket opt-out.
- **Vendor remote access normalised:** VPN connections provided to OEMs and system integrators for ongoing support are often permanently active, unmonitored, and without a prior risk assessment — non-compliant with 2-2-1-7.
- **SIS considered outside cybersecurity scope:** Safety Instrumented Systems are explicitly in scope for OTCC — SIS segmentation (2-4-1-3), SIS controller mode restrictions (2-3-1-5), SIS upload/download monitoring (2-11-1-5), and SIS inclusion in DR plans (2-12-1-5) are all mandatory.
- **Penetration testing scoped to IT only:** Organisations conduct annual IT penetration tests and present these as OTCC evidence. OT/ICS networks must be explicitly in scope, conducted by a qualified OT security team, with passive testing alternatives when production impact is a concern.
- **Vulnerability assessment frequency misread:** The 3/6/12-month cadence based on facility level is a common finding. L1 organisations running quarterly assessments on external systems only — and annual for internal — do not meet the 3-month requirement across all OT/ICS systems.
- **No OT threat intelligence integration:** Organisations feed IT threat feeds into their SIEM but lack OT/ICS-specific threat intelligence (e.g. ICS-CERT, Dragos, Claroty feeds). Control 2-11-1-9 specifically requires OT/ICS threat intelligence to tune SIEM alerts.
- **Engineering files excluded from backup:** Configuration files, ladder logic, engineering drawings, and SIS programming files are frequently excluded from backup scope. Control 2-8-1-2 explicitly requires these to be included.

## Key definitions (from OTCC-1:2022 Appendix A)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Industrial Control System (ICS)** | A collective term for different types of control systems and associated instrumentation — includes all devices, systems, networks, and controls used to operate and/or automate industrial processes |
| **Operational Technology (OT)** | The system of components including network devices, computers, servers, cybersecurity devices, infrastructure equipment, and applications supporting operations, maintenance, monitoring, and cybersecurity of the OT/ICS environment |
| **Critical Facility** | A facility where destruction and/or dysfunction may lead to the disruption or discontinuity of the organisation's operation |
| **Safety Instrumented System (SIS)** | A system designed to bring a process to a safe state when predetermined conditions are violated |
| **Zone** | Grouping of logical or physical assets that share common cybersecurity requirements |
| **Demilitarised Zone (DMZ)** | A perimeter network segment that is logically between internal and external networks |
| **Jump Host** | A single remote access point through which all ingress network traffic must pass between a higher-level zone and a lower-level zone |
| **Choke Point** | A single point through which all incoming and outgoing network traffic is funnelled |
| **Engineering Workstation (EWS)** | Dedicated workstation used for engineering, programming, and configuration of OT/ICS assets |
| **Human-Machine Interface (HMI)** | The interface through which an operator interacts with the OT/ICS environment |
| **Process Hazard Analysis (PHA)** | Organised and systematic assessment of potential hazards associated with an industrial process, addressing known hazards, controls, consequences, facility siting, human factors, and HSE evaluation |
| **Application Whitelisting** | Security practice of specifying an index of approved applications permitted to be present and active on the organisation's machines and servers |
| **Factory Acceptance Test (FAT)** | A test conducted at the vendor facility after assembly and configuration to validate compliance with functional specifications |
| **Site Acceptance Test (SAT)** | A test conducted on-site after installation and configuration to validate compliance with functional specifications and integration with other components |
| **Tabletop Exercise (TTX)** | A simulated discussion-based exercise to test detection and response capabilities through a fictional but realistic OT/ICS cybersecurity event scenario |
| **TTPs** | Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures — the behaviour of a cyber-adversary across the attack lifecycle |
| **User Behaviour Analytics (UBA)** | Tracking, collecting, and analysing user data and patterns to detect harmful or unusual behaviours |
| **RACI Matrix** | Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed Matrix — maps each stakeholder to their degree of involvement in a process |
| **Secure by Design** | A methodology seeking to make systems and networks free from cybersecurity vulnerabilities through continuous testing, authentication safeguards, and adherence to best design practices |
| **Health, Safety, and Environment (HSE)** | The operational domain addressing health, safety, and environmental considerations in industrial and critical infrastructure settings |
